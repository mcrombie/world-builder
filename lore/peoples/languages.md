# Languages of Azhora

Azhora has no single language. It has a continent's worth of them — a tangle of families, dialects, creoles, trade pidgins, and isolated outliers that reflects tens of thousands of years of human movement, separation, contact, and drift. Linguists have spent careers mapping the relationships between them and have achieved broad consensus on some questions and productive argument on many others.

The following is a summary of the major language families, their distributions, and their relationships as currently understood. It is incomplete by necessity; the full picture is a lifetime's work, and the picture is still changing.

## The Mittoli Family

The dominant language family of central and western Azhora, named for the Mittolo region where the prestige dialect developed. Mittoli proper — the standard written form used in commerce, governance, and scholarship — is based on the speech of the Caeras valley cities and has been propagating westward, northward, and partially eastward for several centuries as Mittoli political and economic influence expanded.

The family includes:

**Standard Mittoli** — the prestige dialect. More vowels than consonants. Long sentences. A tendency toward qualification and subordinate clauses that speakers of other families find either precise or exhausting depending on their patience.

**Northern Mittoli** — the dialect of the Bay of Lol and Smunders Bay region. Consonant clusters preserved that simplified in the south. Flatter vowels. Shorter sentences. "Mttolo" where the south says "Mittolo" is a representative example — the unstressed vowel dropped centuries ago in the north and never came back.

**Hill Mittoli** — the Ganoss upland and Orched Hills variant. Considered archaic by lowland speakers. Preserves several grammatical forms that disappeared from standard usage before the Caeras Compact. Linguists find it invaluable; standard Mittoli speakers find it difficult.

**Forest Mittoli** — spoken in the western deep-forest settlements. Has absorbed vocabulary from an unidentified substrate — words for forest phenomena, fungal and root species, certain qualities of light and shadow — that have no cognates in any other language. Where these words came from is an open question.

**Eastern Mittoli** — the transitional dialect of the East Pyros contact zone. Heavily mixed with Pyrosi vocabulary. Sometimes classified as a separate language rather than a dialect; this is a classification argument that generates more heat than light.

## The Pyrosi Family

The language family of the Pyros regions, older than Mittoli as a distinct form, and related to it through a common ancestor that linguists call Proto-Western. The two families diverged sometime before the oldest surviving written records and have been in contact ever since, exchanging vocabulary and structural features while remaining recognizably distinct.

**West Pyrosi** — formal, layered, with a grammar that encodes social relationship in verb forms in ways that cause continuous problems for Mittoli speakers trying to use it. The politeness registers are genuine and non-optional; using the wrong one is not rude, exactly, but it is noticed.

**Taler** — the archaic dialect of the Talermolis upland. Considered by some linguists to be the closest living language to Proto-Western. Taler speakers can occasionally read inscriptions that have no other living interpreters. This is either a minor superpower or a significant burden, depending on how many historians come looking for you.

**East Pyrosi** — the trade dialect, heavily influenced by Iberos coastal language and standard Mittoli. More vocabulary, less grammar, maximum utility. The lingua franca of the transit corridor.

## The Moreshi Family

Completely unrelated to the western families. The Moreshi family represents a separate lineage that predates any contact between the desert and the western continent, and possibly predates the divergence of the western families from their common ancestor. This makes it extremely old and extremely interesting to linguists, and of no practical relevance to anyone trying to get directions in the Moroshé.

The family includes the languages of the settled oasis communities, the nomadic herding cultures, and the canyon country peoples, all of which are related but distinct. The deep desert languages are the most conservative — they have changed less over the centuries, perhaps because isolated communities in harsh terrain have less contact to introduce change. The canyon languages have absorbed some Iberos coastal vocabulary through trade.

There is a register in the oldest Moreshi traditions — used only in certain ritual contexts — that linguists cannot fully analyze. It uses the standard Moreshi sound system but contains syntactic structures that don't match any known Moreshi language. It may be a preserved form of something much older, or it may be a ritual creation designed to be opaque. The desert peoples do not discuss it in terms that help resolve the question.

## The Bouéni Language

The mainland Bouéni language and the related Azner island languages form a small family of their own. Related to Mittoli through an ancient common ancestor but diverged so early that the relationship is visible only through systematic comparison rather than intuition.

The mystery of Bouéni is its substrate — the layer of vocabulary and structure that does not come from the Mittoli ancestor but from something else, something that appears in the oldest forms of the language and cannot be traced to any known family. Bouéni linguists have an explanation for this — they say their ancestors came from the sea — and outside linguists have not been able to disprove it, though they find it unsatisfying.

## The Grassic Family

The Plains languages, forming their own family with no clear relationship to either the western or the desert families. This is the most striking fact in Azhoran linguistics: the people who have lived in the geographic center of the continent for the longest traceable time speak languages with no traceable common origin with anyone around them.

The standard explanation is that the Plains peoples are the oldest population on the continent, predating all the others, and that the other families represent later arrivals whose languages have obscured or replaced whatever was spoken across the continent before them. This would imply that the Grassic family is a remnant of the original Azhoran tongue, which would make the Plains peoples — not the Mittoli, not the desert cultures — the true indigenous population.

This theory is not officially controversial. It is not discussed much in Mittoli academic contexts either.

## Language as Identity

Across all of Azhora, dialect and language function as primary identity markers more reliably than political borders, ethnic categories, or religion. Where you are from is, first and most legibly, how you speak. The name variants on maps — Nyross and Nyrous, Mttolo and Mittolo, Smunders Bay and Smuners Bay — are not errors or inconsistencies. They are evidence of the linguistic texture of the continent: the same places, called different things by different mouths, all of them correct in context, none of them the only right answer.

Scholars who study this note, with some regularity, that the same feature appears in the way Azhorans understand history, politics, and truth. The tendency to hold multiple valid versions simultaneously is not relativism; it is cultural habit developed over centuries of linguistic coexistence. It is also, occasionally, a diplomatic nightmare.
