# Cold Stones

Cold Stones is an archipelago of pale, rocky islands sitting in the cold sea between Azhora's northern coast and the Ice Spear peninsula of the northern continent. It is the furthest north that most Azhoran sailors have gone, and for many expeditions it represents not a destination but the last reliable waypoint before the unknown.

The name distinguishes it from Blue Stones in Bouén — a common source of confusion in navigational records, where "the Stones" without qualification has sent at least two documented supply missions to entirely the wrong place.

## The Islands

Cold Stones comprises roughly thirty islands of notable size and several hundred smaller outcrops and sea-stacks. The larger islands are low and wind-scoured, their surfaces covered in pale rock, hardy coastal grass, and the grey-green lichen that is one of the few things that thrives in the archipelago's conditions. In summer, a thin topsoil supports enough vegetation for seasonal grazing; in winter, the islands are largely stripped bare by winds that come off the northern continent without obstruction.

The rock itself is the feature that gave the islands their name — a pale, almost white sandstone that is unusual for this latitude, where darker igneous rock dominates. The pale stone is visible from considerable distance and gives the archipelago a ghostly quality in low light, particularly in the long winter dusk when the islands glow briefly before going dark.

Ice does not typically form on the islands themselves except in the worst winters, but the sea between them and Ice Spear carries drifting pack ice in all but the warmest months. The channel approach from the south, from Azhora, is clear in summer and difficult in winter.

## Habitation

Cold Stones has been intermittently inhabited for as long as Azhorans have been sailing north. Whether it was inhabited before Azhorans arrived is an open question — certain features of the larger islands, including a series of stone enclosures on the second-largest island that predate any known Azhoran presence, suggest the answer may be yes. These enclosures are not discussed much in the academic literature because it is not clear what they were for, and the field of northern archaeology is underfunded.

Currently, Cold Stones supports two small permanent communities on the largest island — fishing families whose ancestors came from the Smunders Bay coast and who have been here long enough to constitute a distinct sub-culture. They speak a dialect of Northern Mittoli that has drifted considerably from the mainland version and has absorbed vocabulary from sources no one has identified. They are accustomed to hosting Academy expeditions with the specific combination of hospitality and mild contempt that comes from repeatedly accommodating people who think they are the first to notice something you have lived next to your entire life.

## As Waypoint

Cold Stones is the staging ground for any serious attempt on the northern continent. Both Academy expeditions used the largest island as their winter base. The permanent residents have strong opinions about this, which they express through the pricing of supplies and the quality of information they choose to share.

From the northern shore of Cold Stones, Ice Spear is visible on clear days as a pale presence on the horizon, roughly sixty miles away. The water between is cold, often rough, and in winter, heavily iced. The sixty miles might as well be six hundred in terms of the difficulty of crossing them reliably.

## Compass Anomaly

Navigation within and around Cold Stones requires local knowledge because compass needles behave inconsistently in the archipelago. The deviation is not constant — it shifts in ways that are not attributable to any identified magnetic deposit — and it worsens as you approach the northern edge of the archipelago. Navigators who rely on instruments rather than dead reckoning and sky observation have run into trouble here regularly enough that the Academy expedition reports all include warnings about it.

The permanent residents navigate by star and current and do not use compasses. They find the outsiders' reliance on the instruments faintly baffling.
