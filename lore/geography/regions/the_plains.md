# The Plains

The Plains are the transitional zone where the forests of Mittolo thin and die, the soils change, and the land opens into the great rolling grasslands that form the buffer between western and eastern Azhora. They are defined by what they are not: not quite forest, not quite desert, not fully under any one culture's control, never fully belonging to either side of the continent they lie between.

This in-between quality is not a lack. The Plains have their own character, their own peoples, and a history that is in some ways more interesting than the regions that have tried hardest to claim or ignore them.

## Geography

The Plains run as a broad band from north to south across the center of the continent, widest in the middle and narrowing toward the endpoints where the more definite terrains crowd in. The land is gently undulating — not flat in the strict sense, but the rises and falls are long and gradual, and the horizon is always far away. The sky here is larger than anywhere else on Azhora. People who have spent time on the Plains and then return to the forested west or the canyon country of the east consistently report a sense of compression, of the world having become smaller.

The grassland varies in character by latitude and season. In the north it is tough, short-grassed steppe, cold in winter and briefly lush in summer. In the center it reaches its full expression — deep-rooted grasses taller than a person in the wet years, interspersed with wildflower meadows and the occasional stand of isolated trees that mark where an underground water source runs close to the surface. In the south it dries progressively, the grasses becoming sparser and stiffer, until the transition into the Moroshé becomes gradual enough that there is no precise line.

Several rivers cross the Plains, draining from the western uplands to the eastern sea. These rivers do not behave like Mittoli rivers — they are seasonal in the drier stretches, flooding in spring and reducing to chains of pools in late summer. The settlements along them are built accordingly, on high ground with seasonal adjustment.

## The Plains Peoples

The Plains have been inhabited since before the historical record, and the peoples who have lived there longest are not the farmers and city-builders of the neighboring regions but the mobile pastoral cultures who have followed the grass and the seasonal water sources in patterns that the fixed civilizations around them have sometimes understood as wandering and which are, in fact, highly organized systems of land use developed over thousands of years.

The Plains herding cultures move along routes that are as well-established as any Mittoli road, governed by a body of customary law concerning grazing rights, water access, and passage that is more sophisticated than it looks to outsiders because it is oral rather than written. The cultures that have attempted to impose fixed land ownership on the Plains have consistently found that the pastoral peoples had already developed workable solutions to every problem those systems were designed to address, and that the imposition created new problems rather than solving old ones.

The Plains peoples speak several related languages that form their own family, distinct from Mittoli, Pyrosi, and the desert tongues. The family is called Grassic by outside linguists, a name the Plains peoples find mildly insulting and inaccurate, though they have not converged on an alternative.

## The Contested Zone

The Plains are also a major transit corridor, and their middle sections — where the main east-west trade routes run — have been controlled, fought over, taxed, and disputed by the surrounding civilizations in ways that have overlaid the pastoral landscape with a layer of fortifications, abandoned garrison towns, and the ghost infrastructure of competing administrative systems.

The large settled towns of the central Plains exist primarily because of trade, not agriculture. They are cosmopolitan, rough-edged, and full of people who are passing through. The laws that apply in them are a pragmatic composite of whatever the currently dominant regional power has managed to enforce, the customary law of the Plains peoples that everyone actually lives by, and the commercial law of the Iberos coast that governs any transaction significant enough to be written down.

## The Memory Grass

There is a Plains tradition, found across multiple pastoral cultures in slightly different forms, concerning a type of grass called Memory Grass — a grass that is said to retain an impression of what has walked through it, not in the physical sense of bent blades, but in some quality that animals can perceive and that sensitive humans can sometimes feel. Old battle sites on the Plains are said to be readable this way: the horses grow nervous, the dogs circle without settling, and people who sleep there report dreams that are not their own.

The Plains peoples treat these sites as markers rather than hazards. They are mapped in oral tradition, known to every experienced herder, and avoided not out of fear but out of a kind of respect for something that happened and has not entirely stopped happening. Outside scholars who have attempted to study these sites find that the Plains people are cooperative up to a point — they will show you where the sites are, they will tell you what is remembered about what happened there — but they become firm and quiet when asked to explain the mechanism, in the way people become firm and quiet when asked to justify something they know to be true but cannot prove.
