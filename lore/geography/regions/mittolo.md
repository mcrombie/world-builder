# Mittolo

Mittolo is the green heart of Azhora — the broad, forested, river-threaded region that occupies the center and west of the continent and has been the seat of its dominant civilizations for as long as records have been kept. The name itself is old enough that its meaning has been debated by linguists for centuries; the most widely accepted reading traces it to a pre-Compact root meaning something close to "the middle that holds," though the "holds" carries a structural sense — the middle that keeps things together — rather than the possessive. Whether this reflects an ancient geographic self-understanding or is a later rationalization of a word no one fully remembers is unresolved.

## Geography

Mittolo is defined by its rivers. The three great river systems — the Velm in the north, the Caeras through the center, and the slow, wide Ond in the south — drain the interior forests and hill country toward the western coast, and everything that has happened in Mittolo has happened near their banks or along the roads that follow them. The rivers are navigable for most of their length, which meant that goods, armies, and ideas moved freely through the region long before road-building became sophisticated.

The land between the rivers is forested — deeply in the west, more openly in the east where the trees begin to thin toward the transitional zone of the Plains. The forests are old. The western groves in particular contain trees that dendrochronologists cannot fully date; the rings become too compressed, too dense, somewhere past three thousand years. What the forest was before the oldest dateable growth is not known.

The terrain is gently rolling except in the northwest, where the landscape roughens into the hill country that surrounds the Bay of Lol and the Ganoss uplands. Elevation is modest throughout — Mittolo has no dramatic peaks, only long ridges and broad valleys. The absence of hard geographic barriers has historically meant that borders here have been cultural and political rather than natural, and have moved accordingly.

## Climate

Mittolo is temperate and wet. Prevailing winds off the western sea bring reliable rainfall across most of the region for most of the year, with no true dry season. Winters are cold but not severe at lower elevations; the northern reaches approach bitter cold in deep winter. Summers are mild and green.

The forests regulate temperature and humidity in ways that are by now self-sustaining — the canopy moderates extremes, and the deep root systems maintain soil moisture through dry spells. Agriculturally, Mittolo is among the most productive regions on the continent, and has fed the populations of its cities and the ambitions of its rulers for millennia.

## History

The recorded history of Mittolo is the recorded history of Azhora, more or less, which is a Mittoli bias that scholars from other regions have noted with some irritation. The oldest surviving written texts on the continent were produced here. The first political compacts that formed the basis of later governance were drafted along the Caeras. The institutions that became the Azhoran Academy, the system of weights and measures used across most of the continent, and the calendar in common use all originated in the Mittolo heartland.

This has not made Mittolo peaceful. The region's fertility and central position made it a prize, and its history is one of repeated fragmentation, unification, conquest, and re-fragmentation. The current political configuration — a loose set of city-states and regional councils nominally bound by the Second Caeras Compact — is roughly five hundred years old and has survived primarily because no single power within Mittolo has been strong enough to absorb the others, and powers from outside have found it expensive to hold.

The oldest stratum of Mittoli history, before the first written records, is accessible only through archaeology and oral tradition. The archaeology suggests a much earlier period of high organization — coordinated construction, evidence of long-distance trade, grain storage at a scale implying central authority — that ended abruptly several thousand years ago with what the physical record describes as burning and abandonment on a regional scale. What caused this is not established. It is called the First Silence in Mittoli oral tradition, and is described not as a conquest or a catastrophe but as a departure — "the people before us left." Where they went is not part of the tradition.

## Language and Dialect

The Mittoli language family is the dominant linguistic group in central-western Azhora. Standard Mittoli — the prestige dialect used in formal writing, commerce, and cross-regional communication — is based on the speech of the Caeras valley cities. But Mittoli spoken in the northern hill country (where it shades toward the rougher consonants of the Bay of Lol dialect), the deep western forests (where it has absorbed vocabulary from older sources that linguists cannot confidently trace), and the eastern transitional zone (where it mixes with Pyric influences) are all distinct enough to cause regular miscommunication and occasional diplomatic incidents.

The name "Mittolo" itself is pronounced differently in different parts of the region — "mih-TOL-oh" in the cities, "MIT-uh-luh" in the hill country, and "meh-TOL" in the old western forest settlements, clipped and flat.

## The Dark Groves

The dense, dark patches of forest visible across Mittolo — older growth, different in character from the surrounding woodland — are called the Dark Groves by most Mittoli, though each one has its own local name. They are not avoided exactly, but they are treated with a certain care. Hunters do not make camps in them. Children are told not to play in them after dark. The explanations given for this vary: "the trees are too close," "the animals behave strangely," "it is old and the old things don't like noise." None of these explanations are satisfying on examination, and Mittoli people are generally not interested in examining them.

Academic expeditions into the Dark Groves have consistently reported two things: that the interiors are unusually quiet even by dense-forest standards, and that their maps, when compared afterward, consistently show the Groves as slightly smaller than they appeared to be when inside them. This discrepancy has not been explained.
