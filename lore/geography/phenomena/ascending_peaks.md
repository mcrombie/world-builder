# Ascending Peaks

The Ascending Peaks are a cluster of approximately forty mountain summits in the central-northern ranges of Azhora where the rock does not stay down. Sections of cliff face, boulders, and in some cases entire peak caps have detached from the underlying mountain and risen — slowly, over geological time — to hang at various heights above the valleys below.

The effect ranges from subtle to spectacular. The most modest manifestations are boulders hovering two or three feet off the valley floor, rocking gently in wind. The most dramatic is the Floating Crown, the detached summit cap of a peak the locals call Ossian's Head, which hovers approximately 800 feet above where it would otherwise rest, trailing a slow rain of pebbles.

## The Physics

No satisfying explanation has been produced. The Ascending Peaks have attracted more academic expeditions than nearly any other feature on Azhora, and the consensus after two hundred years of investigation is that something is happening here that is not consistent with any known force. The rock is not magnetic. There are no gas pockets or thermal effects that could account for the lift. The hovering sections are in equilibrium — they do not drift, do not accelerate upward, do not descend. They simply stay where they are.

The boundary of the effect is defined. Outside the Ascending Peaks region, rocks fall normally. The boundary is not a sharp line — there is a zone of several miles on the southern and western edges where things fall more slowly than expected, as if the effect grades off. On the northern and eastern edges the cutoff is sharper.

What happens within the boundary with things that are not rock has been studied with considerable interest. Water falls slowly — streams within the region move in long, lazy arcs. Animals brought into the region show no anomalous effects. People do not float. Objects made of metal fall at normal rates. The effect is specific to stone.

## The Mountain Cultures

The cultures of the northern ranges who live in and around the Ascending Peaks have developed entire architectural traditions around the phenomenon. Buildings are constructed to take advantage of stable hovering rock as natural platforms and bridges. The city of Veth Ossian, the largest settlement in the region, is partially built on four large hovering platforms at varying heights above the valley floor, connected by rope bridges and wooden walkways. It is either beautiful or terrifying depending on your tolerance for heights and unusual physics.

The local cultures do not treat the hovering as mysterious — it is simply the nature of their home. The theological explanation varies by community, but the most common holds that this region is where "the world's grip loosened," and that the stone here has partially returned to a prior state. A prior state of what is described differently in different traditions.

## Research Status

The Azhoran Academy of Natural History maintains a permanent station in the Ascending Peaks. It is well-staffed and well-funded relative to most field stations, because the Ascending Peaks are dramatically strange but not dangerous — nothing about them has hurt anyone — and because several wealthy patrons have found the phenomenon fascinating enough to support indefinite investigation.

Current research is focused on determining whether the hovering rock changes mass. Early results suggest it does not. This rules out certain hypotheses and opens others that the researchers have declined to publish in preliminary form because, as one internal letter put it, "we don't want to write the paper before we understand what we're looking at."

Whether the effect is unique to this location on Azhora, or whether similar phenomena exist elsewhere on Corav, is unknown. No reports from sailors or traveling scholars have described anything comparable in the lands they have seen.
