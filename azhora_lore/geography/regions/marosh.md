# Marosh

Marosh is the dominant kingdom of the Dinova Peninsula, and the word "dominant" requires immediate qualification: Marosh does not dominate the peninsula by military occupation, agricultural wealth, or naval power. It dominates by controlling access. The Meroshe desert occupies the interior of the Dinova Peninsula in the way that a landlocked sea occupies an isthmus — it is not an obstacle to be overcome so much as a medium to be managed. The kingdom of Marosh has spent several centuries becoming expert at that management: deciding which caravans cross the Meroshe, in what sequence, at what tariff, and through which routes. Its capital, **Dinelv**, sits on the eastern (Iberos-facing) shore at the junction of the overland road from the desert's edge and the coastal trade lanes. From Dinelv, Marosh administers a kingdom whose territory is partly desert, partly coast, partly nominally subject canyon people, and partly the small commercial state of Hama at the peninsula's southwestern tip — which Marosh needs far more than it will publicly acknowledge.

---

## Geography

The Dinova Peninsula extends southwest from the Azhoran continental mass, flanked on its eastern face by the Iberos Sea and on its western face by the open ocean approaches. The peninsula's interior is occupied almost entirely by the **Meroshe** — the desert whose name shares the same triconsonantal root (M-R-Ṣ) as the broader Moroshé of the continental interior, though the Meroshe's character is shaped by the peninsula's narrower geography and its distance from the deep canyon country further north.

The Meroshe is not uniform. Its coastal margins are semi-arid, Mediterranean in character during the cooler months, with enough seasonal rain to sustain the scrub and the small agricultural plots that feed the coastal towns. Moving inland, the vegetation thins rapidly. The central desert reaches are hot, rocky in the north, more sandy in the south, with the geology shifting again in the deep canyon country of the southwestern interior where the Meroshe descends through layers of exposed sedimentary rock into the canyon systems that the canyon peoples have inhabited for as long as any record survives.

The eastern coast, where Dinelv sits, benefits from the Iberos maritime climate: cooler summers than the interior, wet winters, a harbor adequate for the medium-draft vessels that carry the Iberos coastal trade. The harbor is not excellent — deep-sea vessels prefer Hama's southwestern harbor — but it is serviceable, and Dinelv's position at the road-mouth makes it the natural administrative center regardless of its nautical limitations.

The western face of the peninsula is drier, windier, and navigated by vessels experienced in the open-ocean conditions off the cape. Hama, at the southwestern tip, has the better natural harbor and the better commercial position for trade coming north from the southern archipelago chains. This geographic fact is the central unresolved tension in Marosh's political economy.

## The Desert-Road Kingdom

The caravan routes across the Meroshe are Marosh's revenue base, its military rationale, its diplomatic leverage, and its vulnerability. They connect the Iberos Sea coast with the peninsula's western face, which means they connect the Iberos trade system with the southern archipelago trade system — two commercial worlds that would otherwise have to conduct business by ship around the cape. The overland crossing, despite its costs in water, animals, and tariffs, is faster for many goods and avoids the seasonal navigational difficulties of the cape route.

Marosh's administration of these routes is sophisticated in ways that are sometimes underappreciated by outsiders who see only a desert toll operation. **The Route Registry** at Dinelv maintains detailed records of caravan movements, waystation conditions, water availability at each stage, seasonal variations in route viability, and the reliability of the various intermediary families who manage specific segments. The Registry is updated continuously by riders and by the reports of returning caravaneers. When a water source at a waystation degrades, the Registry notes it and adjusts the recommended loads for the next season's crossing accordingly. When a canyon community that nominally manages a section of route becomes unavailable for reasons the Registry politely does not specify, the Registry notes the alternative routing and the additional days that alternative requires.

What the registry cannot do is replace the knowledge held by the canyon peoples themselves. The deep routes — the ones that cross the canyon country rather than skirting its edges — are not fully mapped by the Maroshi court. The canyon peoples know paths through the rock that Maroshi surveyors have not traced and that the court does not ask about directly, because asking directly would invite an explicit refusal that would then have to be managed. The court's preference is to maintain the fiction that the canyon routes are under Maroshi jurisdiction while operationally treating the canyon communities as autonomous contractors. This fiction is maintained by both parties. The canyon peoples find it acceptable because it leaves them alone; the Maroshi court finds it acceptable because disrupting it would cost more than sustaining it.

Disruptions to trans-desert traffic produce immediate fiscal pressure at Dinelv. The kingdom has limited agricultural reserves, no significant domestic manufacture, and a court that has calibrated its expenditures to a steady tariff income. When a caravan season is poor — through drought, route closure, political disruption in the source territories, or a Haman commercial dispute that redirects goods to the sea route — the treasury feels it within one season and the court feels it within two.

## The Court at Dinelv

The Maroshi court is a desert-economics court. Its administrative competence runs to route management, water logistics, tariff calculation, caravaner relations, and the dense diplomatic correspondence required to manage relationships with the Pyrosi border territories to the north, the canyon communities to the interior, and Hama to the south. It is not a court that thinks primarily in terms of agriculture, naval power, or territorial occupation. These are not its instruments.

The court language is Maroshi, a dialect of **Coastal Trade Moreshi** — the triconsonantal-root language family that descends from the older Moreshi spoken across the desert interior. Coastal Trade Moreshi developed in the ports and caravan termini as a commercial and administrative register; Maroshi is its eastern-coast peninsular form. The court employs the language's formal registers for treaty correspondence and tariff documentation with a precision that experienced traders recognize as professional. The commercial vocabulary is extensive. The vocabulary for territorial governance — land tenure, agricultural rights, the administrative structures of settled populations — is borrowed and approximate, because Marosh has not historically needed it for its core function.

This is the court's structural limitation. Its sophistication is genuine but domain-specific. The administrators who manage the Route Registry with precision are the same administrators who write policy toward the canyon peoples that reveals, in almost every clause, that the court does not fully understand what it is administering. They know the routes. They know the tariffs. They know which waystation families can be trusted and which require the presence of an official escort. What they do not know — and what the court's institutional culture does not encourage asking about — is the canyon peoples' own political structure, their internal hierarchies, their historical claims, their relationship to the specific geography they inhabit. The court treats them as route-management infrastructure and is consistently surprised when they act otherwise.

## The Hama Question

Hama presents the Maroshi court with a problem it has never fully resolved: a subordinate that cannot be replaced.

Marosh's nominal relationship to Hama is one of overlordship. Hama pays tribute and operates under arrangements that the Dinelv court characterizes as access permissions rather than recognized sovereignty. This characterization is not entirely false — Hama does pay tribute, and the arrangements do exist. What the characterization omits is that the arrangements run both ways: Marosh's ability to collect the tribute depends on Hama's commercial networks remaining functional, which they do because Hama's **Council of Merchant Houses** knows how to run them and the Dinelv court does not.

The southern archipelago trade — the goods moving north from the island chains beyond the peninsula's tip — arrives at Hama because Hama's harbor is excellent for deep-sea vessels and because the Galan merchant networks that manage the northern end of that trade have relationships with specific Haman houses that have taken generations to build. Those relationships are not transferable. A Maroshi official appointed to supervise Hama's commerce cannot activate those networks; he can only observe them and take his percentage. If the Maroshi court presses too hard — raises tribute, attempts to replace Haman commercial officials with Dinelv appointees, or tries to redirect the southern trade through the eastern harbor — the Galan merchants do not argue. They find other arrangements. The goods stop moving. The Dinelv treasury notes the shortfall.

Maroshi kings who understand this dynamic govern Hama through light presence: tribute at a rate that Haman houses can sustain without restructuring their operations, a Dinelv observer resident in the council quarter, and formal acknowledgments of Maroshi precedence in the public buildings that the Dinelv court monitors for symbolic compliance. Kings who do not understand this dynamic attempt closer control, damage the commercial networks they depend on, and are remembered in the Registry's marginal notes for the years when the route income was low.

## The Canyon Peoples

The deep Meroshe supports communities that have lived in the canyon systems since well before Marosh existed as a political entity. They are Moreshi-speaking — a canyon dialect of the triconsonantal family, more conservative in its root structure than the Coastal Trade registers and with a distinct phonology that marks the [r]/[ʀ] distinction the Coastal registers have collapsed. Their relationship to the desert's geology is expressed in the **Qadimuur** ritual register, the archaic speech reserved for address to the ruins and the ancient presence acknowledged in the oldest rock.

The canyon peoples are nominally subject to Marosh. In practice, this means that Marosh collects no tax from them directly, maintains no garrison in the canyon country, and administers them through the same intermediary families who manage the route segments. The intermediaries are typically canyon-dwelling families with enough Coastal Trade Moreshi to communicate with Dinelv and enough canyon standing to communicate with the deeper communities. They serve both sides without fully serving either, which is a sustainable arrangement because both sides find it preferable to the alternative.

What the canyon peoples will not discuss with the Maroshi court — or with outside researchers, or with the caravaneers who cross their territory — is the character of the ancient presence in the oldest formations. The Qadimuur register is not accessible to outsiders, and the ruins that predate the canyon communities' own oral histories are approached in silence. The Dinelv court's Route Registry notes certain canyon sections as "administered through local arrangement" without specifying what the arrangement covers. Experienced Registry officials understand that asking what it covers is inadvisable.

## Commerce and Tariffs

The Maroshi tariff system is the most detailed commercial regulatory framework on the southern peninsula and arguably one of the most detailed on the continent. Tariff rates vary by cargo type, route segment, season, caravan size, origin territory, and the current political relationship between Marosh and the source kingdom. The **Tariff Table** is renegotiated in full every ten years and amended continuously between negotiations.

Caravaneers who cross the Meroshe regularly develop relationships with the Registry officials who process their papers. These relationships are not corrupt in the sense of bypassing the tariff — the tariff is too central to Maroshi revenue for the court to tolerate systematic evasion, and the punishments for falsified manifests are severe and consistently applied. What the relationships provide is predictability: a known Registry official applies the table consistently, explains ambiguous classifications in the caravaneer's favor when the classification is genuinely ambiguous, and processes the paperwork efficiently. This has commercial value that experienced caravaneers factor into their route decisions.

The tariff also applies to the water and supply depots along the route. Waystations charge regulated rates for water, fodder, and basic repair. These rates are set by the Registry and enforced, but the condition of the stations varies with the season and the local family's current relationship with Dinelv. A waystation where the managing family is in good standing with the court is well-maintained; a waystation whose family is navigating a tribute dispute with Dinelv may be adequately stocked but is not going to volunteer the extra water reserve it keeps for emergencies. Experienced caravaneers know which stations to trust in which seasons and plan their loads accordingly.

The caravan economy is the skeleton around which Marosh is built. Without it, Dinelv is a moderate coastal town on the Iberos shore, and the Meroshe is simply an obstacle. With it, Dinelv is the administrative center of a revenue system that extracts value from the geographic fact of the peninsula's width. Marosh knows this. The court's calendar, its diplomatic year, its budget cycle — all of them follow the caravan seasons. The kingdom is, in the most literal sense, a road kingdom: not a territory so much as a corridor, and not a ruler so much as a toll.
