# Nothwood

The Nothwood is the northern limit of continuous forest cover on Azhora. Below it, to the south and east, the forest extends unbroken through the Acorwood and the mixed woodlands of the northern interior; above it, to the north, the trees grow thinner and shorter and further apart until they stop, and what begins is the open upland country of the Noth Hills and the approach to Cape Thalmagar. The Nothwood is not where the Ibenwood's density begins, nor where the Acorwood's particular ancient character starts. It is its own thing: a cold-adapted northern forest that shares the structural vocabulary of boreal woodlands across the continent but has developed its own specific character in this specific latitude, in this specific cold, with this specific relation to the open country above and the mixed forest below.

The name is outside-origin — the Northern Azhoran coastal peoples and the Lond-country farmers named it as the northern wood, which is what it is in relation to them, and the name has propagated into general use without reflecting how the people who live in it think of it, if they think of it as a named thing at all. The forest floor does not announce itself as the Nothwood. It announces itself as particular trees on particular ground in particular weather, and the people who inhabit it work in those particulars.

## Geography

The Nothwood occupies the ground between the Noth Hills' southern edge and the transitional zone where the northern boreal gives way to the mixed forest that spreads through the Acorwood approaches and the broader Azhoran interior. This transition — from Nothwood to Acorwood margin — is not a clean line. The character changes gradually and unevenly, with the boreal species retreating on the warmer south-facing slopes before the north-facing hollows, and the mixed-forest species advancing in a pattern that the Academy's botanists have been documenting on the theory that it is advancing — that the Nothwood is slowly shrinking from below as the climate modifies at timescales longer than individual human attention but shorter than geological time.

The forest does not cover its territory uniformly. There are clearings where the soil is too thin and the rock too close to the surface for the trees to establish, and these openings are the reference points that the people who know the Nothwood use for orientation — they are the places where the sky is visible, where the direction of the prevailing weather becomes legible, where the wider terrain can be read before the canopy closes again. The forest that closes between the clearings is the dominant condition: dense enough, in its central zone, that the canopy blocks most of the available light and the forest floor is in a perpetual filtered dimness that is not darkness but is not, precisely, light either.

The topography beneath the forest is not flat. The Nothwood sits on the southern slopes of the same upland terrain system that produces the Noth Hills — rounded ridges, shallow basins, the drainage complex of a landscape that was glaciated and has been draining slowly since. The streams that flow south through the Nothwood are cold and fast and reliable in the sense that they exist where the drainage requires them; their exact courses are not reliable, as the log-jams that the forests produce are moved by high-water events that redirect channels over timescales of decades. A path that followed a particular stream into the Nothwood a generation ago may not follow the same watercourse today.

The northern margin of the Nothwood — where the forest meets the Noth Hills — is among the more complex transition zones in northern Azhora. The trees at this edge are **northern spruce** and **balsam fir** in their most wind-compressed and cold-adapted forms: short, dense, their crowns swept into the aerodynamic shapes that persistent wind produces in trees that survive by presenting the minimum surface to the force trying to break them. Individual trees at the extreme northern margin of the forest are decades old and less than two meters tall. They are not saplings. They are mature trees growing at the limit of what the climate allows, doing it by being small.

## The Forest Character

The Nothwood's dominant species are **northern spruce**, **balsam fir**, and — in the lower, slightly warmer ground along the southern boundary and the watercourses — stands of **northern birch** whose white bark reads as the most visible feature in the forest at any season. The birch is not primarily ornamental. In a forest where most of the resources require either height or patience, the birch provides what small tools require, on timescales that fit within human planning horizons. The bark works; the wood works; the sap in early spring is available and nourishing. The people who live in the Nothwood have uses for all three that the people south of it do not need to know.

The spruce and fir that dominate are not of the size or apparent antiquity that the Ibenwood achieves; they are northern-scale trees, their growth limited by the short seasons and the cold that arrives early and stays late. But the forest is old in the sense of having been forest for a long time — the same general species assemblage occupying the same general ground for longer than any documented record. What changes is the specific location of the canopy, the specific distribution of the clearings, the specific course of the streams; what does not change is the forest's presence on this ground.

The forest floor is a specific environment. In the spruce and fir zones, the canopy intercepts so much of the available light that the understory is limited to species adapted to low light: mosses that carpet every horizontal surface, a few shade-tolerant ferns along the watercourses, the occasional **northern wintergreen** in small-leaved clusters that sit close to the ground and accumulate the cold. In the birch zones, the understory is more complex — the birch canopy is lighter and more variable through the seasons, and below it there is a shrub layer of **northern willow** and **dwarf alder** in the wetter ground that provides food for animals and material for people.

The forest moves in weather in ways that the outside traveler, accustomed to more open country, may not have calibrated before encountering it. Wind that is moderate at the forest edge becomes an auditory event in the canopy — a sustained roaring from above, the crowns swaying while the trunks remain motionless, the light on the forest floor flickering with the movement. This, combined with the compass unreliability that the iron-rich substrate produces in parts of the Nothwood and the featureless similarity of the forest floor across large areas, makes orientation in the Nothwood a practical skill rather than a casual assumption.

## Peoples and Settlements

The Nothwood is inhabited, but its inhabitants are not easily found by people who arrive without prior relationship. The settlements are small — a dozen to at most forty or fifty people — and they are sited for the practical requirements of cold-climate forest habitation: water access, defensible positioning above the drainage hollows where cold air pools, proximity to the clearings that allow winter-sky visibility, and a specific relationship to the game trails and fish runs that provide the primary food supply. They are not near the forest edge by design. The people who have chosen to live in the Nothwood have generally chosen not to be readily accessible to whoever is moving along the forest edge.

The communities in the Nothwood do not appear in northern Azhoran administrative records with any consistency. They trade with the Lond-country and Henborth communities in a selective way — specific goods, specific relationships, not a systematic commercial engagement — and they receive travelers who arrive in the right manner and with appropriate purpose. What "appropriate purpose" means is not written down anywhere accessible to the arriving traveler; it is legible to the people of the Nothwood by how the arriving person presents themselves and how they navigate the first contacts, which are not always immediately recognizable as such.

From the outside, the Nothwood communities appear homogeneous. From within, they are differentiated by the specific geography of their settlements — which watercourse, which clearing complex, which section of the forest their knowledge and their resource claims extend through — and by the relationships between settlements, which are long-established and not explained to outsiders because there is no circumstance in which an outsider's understanding of them would be relevant. The settlements cooperate where cooperation is necessary and maintain their own sovereignty otherwise. No outside power has successfully administered the Nothwood interior.

The language of the Nothwood communities is Northern Mittoli of a strongly local character, with substrate elements in the technical vocabulary for forest navigation, forest resources, and weather prediction that are denser than the substrate layer in the Lond or Henborth speech. Some of this substrate vocabulary is shared with Forest Mittoli, which suggests very long forest habitation by populations that share deep ancestry with the Ibenwood peoples. Whether this connection is historically meaningful or is simply the convergent vocabulary of people who live in forests is a question the Nothwood communities have not been asked in a form that produced an answer.

## Orientation and Movement

The difficulty of orientation in the Nothwood is not metaphorical and is not simply a matter of unfamiliarity. The forest floor in the spruce-fir zone offers few distinguishing features at ground level: the same grey-brown bark columns, the same moss surface, the same limited understory, the same filtered light from above. An experienced northern-forest traveler can work with sun angle and the directional growth characteristics of the lichens on the north-facing sides of trunks; the Nothwood's latitude makes sun angle a reliable indicator for fewer hours per day in the dark half of the year than further south, and the lichen indicators work but require attention that most travelers in unfamiliar forest do not maintain consistently.

The watercourses are the reliable navigation tool, and the Nothwood travelers who know what they are doing follow drainage — either upstream toward the hills or downstream toward the Acorwood margins — and use the confluences and the clearings at the watercourse margins as orientation points. This is not the fastest route between two points, but it is the route that does not require the traveler to hold a heading by dead reckoning across featureless terrain where the ground's irregularity and the course corrections imposed by bogs and windfalls compound into errors that close the margin of error quickly.

The people of the Nothwood do not use maps. They have been asked about this by outsiders who arrived with them and found them inaccessible as tools in this context. The Nothwood communities navigate by the same spatial knowledge system as other cold-climate forest peoples: a network of named points and named relationships, held in memory and oriented by movement rather than abstracted onto surface. The names are in their own substrate vocabulary; the Northern Mittoli approximations that have reached outside accounts describe locations in terms that outside travelers find unusable because the descriptions reference other named points whose names and locations are not known to the outsider, producing a recursive navigation system that is complete for the person inside it and opaque for the person outside it.

## Resources

The Nothwood's resources are specific to cold-climate boreal forest and are held and used by the communities that live there. From outside, the forest offers the timber that northern Azhoran builders want for specific applications — the straight, cold-adapted spruce that resists checking in the freeze-thaw cycles that Lond-country construction must endure, the fir that works for particular joinery, the birch that is the primary material for small-scale work across the northern economy. The timber trade between the Nothwood and the Lond and Henborth communities is real and established, but it is conducted on the Nothwood communities' terms: they bring the timber out, or they allow access at specific locations by specific parties with established relationships. Unsanctioned entry for timber cutting has a history that the Nothwood communities have managed in ways that were effective enough to stop establishing precedent after a few early attempts.

Within the forest, the communities exploit a resource base that most outside observers underestimate. The fur-bearing animals of the Nothwood — **northern marten**, **forest lynx**, the occasional wolverine that crosses from further north — are significant in quality if not in quantity. The fish runs in the watercourses provide a reliable cold-season food source if the access points are known. The plant resources — medicinal plants whose specific efficacy in cold-climate applications is not reproducible from southern-latitude versions, bark products, the specific mosses used in wound treatment and cold-weather insulation — circulate through the northern Azhoran economy at low volume and high value in ways that the Nothwood communities manage carefully. They are not interested in sharing sourcing knowledge, which would make them irrelevant to the supply chain.

## Connection to Thalmagar and Lond

The Nothwood is not a barrier between the Lond plateau and Cape Thalmagar. It is a passage, for those who know how to move through it. The Thalmagar, in their seasonal movements between the cape and the Noth Hills, cross the Nothwood's northern margin — or more precisely, they use specific routes through the margin that have been maintained by this movement for generations. The Thalmagar do not spend extended time in the Nothwood interior; the forest is not their characteristic terrain, and the relationship between the Thalmagar and the Nothwood communities is one of mutual awareness and selective engagement rather than the close cohabitation that the Thalmagar maintain with their megafauna.

From Lond's perspective, the Nothwood is the northern edge of the territory that Lond's communities consider within their practical sphere. The Nothwood communities trade with Lond; the Lond farmers know roughly where the forest begins and have generational relationships with specific forest-edge communities that smooth the timber and medicinal-plant trade. This relationship is practical and does not require the Lond farmers to understand the Nothwood interior, which they do not. The forest, to Lond, is the northern edge of the known world — the last place before the country that the Thalmagar know and the Lond farmers do not.

What passes through the Nothwood between Lond and Thalmagar — materially, informationally, in terms of the people who make the connection — is not well documented from outside. The Nothwood communities that occupy the relevant transit zone know what passes through and have their own accounting of it, conducted in their own vocabulary, for their own purposes. That accounting is not available to outside observers, which is not an oversight.
