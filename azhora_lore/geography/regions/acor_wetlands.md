# Acor Wetlands

The **Acor Wetlands** lie along the western margin of the Acorwood, where several slow-moving streams draining the forest's interior edge converge on a basin that the forest's own water retention has kept permanently wet. They are smaller than the Nethermere and less dramatically seasonal — no annual flood cycle that rewrites the landscape, no *haethoss* flood line demarcating the permanently elevated from the periodically drowned, no Flood Council managing the cycle's social consequences. The Acor Wetlands simply sit in their basin and are wet, all of the time, year after year, in the particular way that a place downstream of an old forest with high water retention and no efficient drainage outlet is wet: not spectacularly, not cyclically, but persistently, reliably, and with the specific character of a landscape that has been soaking in its own moisture for long enough to develop into something that resists simplification. The Acorwood peoples who work the wetlands' edge know this landscape in the way that useful knowledge is known: specifically, practically, and without particular interest in explaining it to people who are not going to come here.

---

## Geography

The basin in which the Acor Wetlands sit is not dramatic topography. There is no sudden transition from upland to wetland, no clear geological boundary where the land decides to become marsh. The descent is gradual: the Acorwood's floor levels out at the western edge of its main formation, the soil changes from the dark leaf-mold of the forest interior to a grey-brown clay that holds rather than drains water, and the streams that drain westward out of the forest fan out and slow and begin to pool before they can organize themselves into a single channel running toward the Mithala plain.

The primary streams — the **Acor**, the **Velsond**, and several smaller tributaries without consistent names in the Azhoran record — enter the basin from the east and northeast. The Acor, the largest, has given its name to the whole system. Whether the forest was named for the stream or the stream for the forest is not recoverable from the record; the same root appears in both names and in the names of the communities that work the wetlands' edge, and the question of origin, if it was ever answerable, has been dissolved in three thousand years of common use.

The basin's western edge is approximately defined by a low clay ridge — barely noticeable as topography from above, but functional as a watershed divide that holds the basin's water on the Acorwood side rather than letting it drain toward the Mithala river system. The ridge is not high enough to be seen as a feature; it is simply the point where the land stops descending. Water that accumulates on the Acorwood side of this low divide stays in the wetland basin indefinitely.

The wetlands proper — the permanently inundated or saturated zones — occupy the basin's center. The margin is a complex gradient: reed beds, sedge meadow, willow and alder carr, and the wet forest edge where the Acorwood's characteristic trees begin but grow on the slightly elevated hummocks that keep their roots out of permanent saturation. The gradient does not have a clear inner and outer boundary. It has a center that is clearly wet and a periphery that is clearly not wet and a substantial zone between them that is ambiguous in different ways at different seasons, different rainfall years, and different points along the basin's length.

## The Wetland Ecology

The Acor Wetlands support a distinct ecological community that differs from both the Acorwood interior and the open grassland and arable land that the Mithala plain represents. The reed beds that cover the permanently inundated zone are dominated by the **Acor reed** — the same species that gives the place its name in the folk etymology that northern Azhoran peoples use, though whether the reed naming preceded or followed the place naming is another question without a recoverable answer. The Acor reed is specific: a heavy-stemmed variety that grows taller than its Nethermere equivalent, stores more starch, and produces a harder, more weather-resistant stem. The reed cutters who work the Acor Wetlands distinguish it from other wetland reed species by touch and know the quality variation across the basin's zones — the deepest permanent-water reed is the heaviest and hardest; the shallower marginal reed is lighter and more flexible; the zone between them produces the mixture that the northern coastal construction trade specifically wants.

The waterbird population of the Acor Wetlands is substantial. The basin sits at the intersection of several migratory routes, and the permanent wet habitat provides the specific combination of feeding and nesting resources that waterbirds require. The species composition is not the same as the Nethermere — the Acor Wetlands' permanence and the Acorwood's influence on their character produce a different community than the seasonally expanding and contracting Nethermere basin — but the density is comparable, and the specific species that require dense reed cover rather than open water concentrate in the Acor Wetlands in numbers that observers from outside the region find striking.

The fish fauna of the Acor Wetlands is characteristic of slow, heavily vegetated water: pike, perch, several species of carp, and eel in quantities that the wetlands' edge communities harvest extensively. The eel harvest is the most commercially significant — the Acor eel is known by name in the Mithala distributary markets and in the northern Iberos coast markets that value the specific preparation methods the Acorwood peoples use. The eel runs through the wetlands are managed — specific channels maintained for movement, specific areas left unfished in rotation — through community practice old enough that it is not described as management but as how things are done.

## Peoples and Economy

The communities that work the Acor Wetlands' edge are Acorwood peoples — part of the broader category of northern Azhoran forest peoples that the Acorwood supports, sharing language roots, material culture, and the specific forest orientation that makes outside administrative authorities consistently underestimate how organized and how self-sufficient they are. They are not the same as the deep-forest communities of the Acorwood's interior; they are the edge communities, adapted to the specific combination of forest and wetland resources that the basin margin provides, and they work both zones in seasonal rotation.

The primary wetland harvests are three: reeds, fish, and specific plants. The reed harvest is conducted in the late season after the growth cycle is complete, using boats built for the specific conditions of the Acor Wetlands — flat-bottomed, shallow-drafted, poled rather than rowed where the depth is insufficient for oars, and with the specific structural features for handling large bundles of cut reed that the wetlands' edge boat-builders have refined over generations. The harvest teams go into the reed beds on routes that have been worked for long enough to be known without discussion: which zones yield which quality, where the channels through the beds run that allow a loaded boat to return, where the problematic mud patches are that will sink a heavily loaded boat working the wrong line through the deep zone.

The reed harvest goes north. The **northern coastal peoples** — the Witherst coastal communities and the settlements along the exposed northern Azhoran coast — use Acor reed extensively in construction, for thatching and insulation in the climate-specific combination of wet-cold that their coastal exposure creates. The Acor reed's specific properties — the hardness of its stem, its resistance to water damage after proper curing — make it preferable for this climate to the softer reeds available in coastal marshes that these communities could access more easily. The trade has been going long enough that the Acor Wetlands communities know what grade of reed the northern construction trade wants and cut to that specification rather than to a generic standard.

The plant harvest — primarily the specific aquatic and semi-aquatic plants used for food, medicine, and fiber — is conducted by smaller groups, often single families, working specific zones of the basin that they have maintained relationships with across generations. Several of these plants have no equivalent in the Mithala agricultural economy and move into the Mithala market as specialty goods: the dried tuber of the **marsh reedmace** that northern cooks use as a flour supplement in the specific preparations that have grown up around it, the specific sedge fiber that the Acorwood peoples use for matting and that has a quality advantage over common sedge fiber because it is harvested from the dense permanent-water zone rather than the drier margins.

## The Acorwood Connection

The Acor Wetlands cannot be understood separately from the Acorwood. The wetlands exist because the forest exists — without the forest's water retention, the basin would drain or dry seasonally rather than maintaining its permanent saturation. The specific relationship between the deep Acorwood interior, its drainage behavior, and the wetland basin is not something that the communities work explain in technical terms; it is simply understood as part of the character of the place.

The Acorwood peoples' relationship to the wetlands is not identical to their relationship to the forest interior. The forest interior has its own knowledge system, its own navigational protocols, its own zones of restricted or regulated use. The wetlands are more practically accessible in the sense that the edge communities work them commercially and allow the Mithala river communities limited access for specific purposes. But the wetlands share with the forest interior the quality of being legible to people who know them and illegible to people who do not — not because they conceal their geography from outsiders, but because reading the basin's terrain, water behavior, and seasonal state requires a knowledge that accumulates through experience and cannot be acquired from outside it.

The communities treat the Acorwood and the wetlands as a single ecological zone. The harvest cycles of the two feed into each other: the charcoal production from forest wood supplement the wetlands reed income; the specific forest herbs that grow on the wet-margin of the Acorwood feed into the same market channels as the wetlands plants. The people who know the reed zones are often the same people who know the forest edge. The boundary between the two, in their practical orientation, is not a significant boundary.

The **Endevorfolk** to the east — the shale-highland peoples with their Vornheld epic tradition and their observations of the Acorwood's western face — have a peripheral relationship to the Acor Wetlands that is primarily navigational and mythological rather than economic. The Acorwood's western face, including the wet margin zone, is part of the visual horizon from the Endevor shale country, and the smoke-reading from that face that Endevor tradition practices is part of how the Endevorfolk read the season's character. Whether the Acorwood peoples are aware of being read this way is not established; they are aware of the Endevorfolk's existence and of the eastern horizon's orientation toward their territory, and they are characteristically unrevealing about what they make of the attention.

## Accessibility

The Acor Wetlands are not inaccessible to outsiders in the absolute sense. The western margin — the low clay ridge that marks the basin's boundary toward the Mithala plain — can be reached by anyone who knows to walk in the right direction from the northern Mithala communities. The difficulty is not reaching the edge; it is what comes next. The wetlands' interior is navigable only in the flat-bottomed shallow-draft boats that the Acorwood edge communities build and use. A person in a standard river boat will not go far before the water becomes too shallow and the vegetation too dense. A person on foot will not go far before the substrate becomes unsafe to walk on — the surface of the permanent-water zone has a specific quality that the communities describe with a single word in their internal language and that translates roughly as "not-solid ground that looks like solid ground."

The channels that allow navigation through the reed beds exist but are not marked. They shift seasonally as the reed growth moves and as the basin's water level rises and falls. A person who does not know where the channels are cannot find them by visual inspection from a boat in the reed zone because the reed closes over everything at head height and the channel is a gap in the reed that looks, from inside the reed bed, identical to a gap that ends in mud. The communities know the channels. They are not recorded on any external chart. They are used by the people who know them and not used by anyone else.

The Mithala administrative tradition has noted the Acor Wetlands in several periods as an area of uncertain access that resists conventional administrative reach, and this notation has been accurate in each period it was made. The Mithala river-plain communities in the northern Lizeem distributary zones maintain trade relationships with the Acorwood edge communities, including those who work the wetlands, but these relationships are commercial rather than administrative. The grain and pottery that move west from the Mithala in exchange for reeds, fish, and wetlands products pass through commercial channels that both parties understand. The administrative relationships — tax, levy, census — have not followed the commercial relationships into the wetlands, and the wetlands communities have not found it necessary to explain why.

## Resources

The Acor Wetlands' resource profile is specific enough to have generated consistent outside commercial interest without generating outside territorial control, because the resources require the kind of knowledge to access and process that cannot be acquired from the outside on a schedule useful for administration. The reed trade is the most visible externally; it is also the most resistant to outside extraction because the reed's quality depends on knowing which zones to harvest, in which season, using which preparation methods, and the knowledge of all three resides in the Acorwood edge communities exclusively.

The eel trade is the second most visible, with the Acor eel's commercial reputation creating demand in markets that reach as far as the Iberos coast. What the Iberos coast markets receive is the cured and prepared product, processed by the wetlands communities using methods developed for the specific characteristics of the Acor eel population. The same eel, removed from the wetlands and processed by outside hands, is not the same product; the communities who have tried to replicate the preparation without the specific knowledge of the source material have produced inferior results that the markets can distinguish. This quality dependence maintains the wetlands communities' commercial position without requiring any formal protection of their methods, because the methods are embedded in knowledge that cannot be separated from the people who hold it.

The medicinal plant trade is smaller in volume but higher in value per unit. The specific aquatic plants of the Acor Wetlands appear in the Mithala plain trade as components of preparations used by the inland communities for purposes that the Acorwood peoples can supply but do not explain in full. The buyers have learned what to ask for and what it does; they have not learned, and the wetlands communities have not offered to teach them, what conditions produce the quality variation between batches. This is not deliberate concealment on the communities' part. It is simply that the knowledge of conditions includes being there and reading the basin the way the basin is readable, and that cannot be transferred in a transaction.
