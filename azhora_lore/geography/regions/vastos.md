# Vastos

**Vastos** is the kind of territory that administrative powers describe as a problem and the people who live there describe as a place. The broad upland plain east of the Pyros territories and west of the Elagosi lake country occupies a position on the map that makes political sense — it connects two major cultural spheres, sits between the volcanic west and the lacustrine east, and is large enough to matter as a territorial claim — and almost no practical sense. The terrain is high, cold-climate grassland, good for the specific livestock that cold-climate grasslands produce and not well-suited to the road-building, grain taxation, and centralized administration that either the Pyrosi or the Elagosi have consistently tried to impose on it. Both traditions have administered Vastos at various points; both have found it an unrewarding experience. The Vastos communities, who have watched this process repeat enough times to recognize its phases, treat the periodic arrival of administrative ambition with the patience of people who have done the arithmetic and know how it resolves.

---

## Geography

The Vastos plain is genuinely flat by highland standards — a broad elevated tableland that sits above the surrounding terrain on both its eastern and western approaches, reached by gradual climbs from the Pyros foothills in the west and from the lake-country margins in the east. This elevation gives Vastos its cold climate and its open, exposed character. There is no significant sheltering terrain: the sky is large, the wind is consistent, and the grass, which is Vastos's primary resource, grows in the dense cold-adapted varieties that upland grazing requires.

**The Vastos River** — the territory's defining drainage — runs roughly east to west across the plain's southern portion, fed by snow melt from the higher ground at the margins and supplemented by the small tributary streams that cross the plain from north to south. It is not a large river in the lowland sense: it is relatively shallow, braided in its middle sections where the flat terrain slows it, and subject to dramatic seasonal variation that makes it both a water source and a periodic obstacle depending on the year. The communities near the river have developed specific management practices for the flood periods that differ from the open-range management of the central plain. The river's crossing points have been contested at various times in Vastos's administrative history, because whoever controls the principal crossings controls movement across the plain.

The margins of Vastos are more complex than the plain interior. The western edge, where the Vastos upland begins to descend toward the Pyros foothills, has the volcanic-influence terrain that is characteristic of the Pyros transition zone — the residual geothermal ground, the occasional sulfur spring, the rock types that the Pyrosi identify as meaningful even when they are not actively fumarolic. These western-margin communities have more cultural contact with the Pyrosi and more mixed identities than the central plain communities. The eastern edge, descending toward the lake country, is moister and more varied, with some small lake basins that prefigure the Elagosi landscape beyond. The communities there have more contact with Elagosi trade networks and have absorbed more Elagosi administrative vocabulary.

## The Pyrosi-Elagosi Margin

The political geography of Vastos is shaped by its position between two major traditions, neither of which has a strong natural claim to it. The Pyrosi interest in Vastos is primarily strategic: the upland plain provides a territorial buffer and a potential recruitment zone, and Pyrosi communities in the volcanic heartland have always found the cold grassland east somewhat alien but worth controlling. The Elagosi interest, channeled primarily through **Ambron**, is more explicitly administrative: the empire's reach tends to follow water, and Vastos's river system, however modest, represents a connectivity claim that Ambronite prefects have periodically tried to formalize into tax structures.

What neither tradition accounts for in its administrative calculations is the specific character of Vastos's terrain. The plain resists road-building in the way that flat, frost-heaved, seasonally saturated upland ground resists road-building: the roads built in drier periods fail in wet ones, the bridges at the Vastos River crossings require more maintenance than the tax revenue from the region justifies, and the supply lines that connect Vastos to either administrative center are long enough to make military presence expensive. Pyrosi garrison forces in Vastos historically spend more time maintaining their own provisioning than enforcing Pyrosi administrative authority. Ambronite prefectures in Vastos have a characteristic documentary record: extremely active in the first decade of their establishment, progressively thinner over the following generations, and ending with a final period in which the prefectural correspondence is almost entirely requests for resources that the center cannot afford to send.

The Vastos communities watch both dynamics with an equanimity that is not passive. They cooperate with administrative authority where cooperation costs little and yields access to trade networks, legal frameworks, or physical security. They do not cooperate where cooperation would require restructuring the pastoral practices that the land requires. The Pyrosi have tried to reorganize Vastos grazing management around fire-centered practices that do not translate to Vastos conditions. The Elagosi have tried to impose grain quotas on land that does not reliably produce grain. Neither attempt has been sustained past the point where the administering power was willing to enforce it by maintaining a permanent military presence, which is rarely past the first generation of administration.

## The Vastos Pastoral Tradition

The Vastos people are, in their own terms, a cattle and sheep culture. The **Vastos longhorn** — a large, cold-tolerant breed with a constitution suited to open upland grazing and severe winters — is the foundation of the economy and the primary unit of social accounting. Disputes are settled in cattle. Marriages are contracted in cattle. The status distinctions that matter in Vastos communities are distinctions between people who manage herds differently, who breed toward different qualities, who control different seasonal grazing routes.

The **vel-vastos** — the Mittoli-contact word that Vastos communities use among themselves for the system of seasonal movement that structures their year — is not a casual practice. The routes that move herds from winter lowlands to summer upland, the management of the small watering points across the plain, the timing of the river crossings relative to the spring flood — all of this constitutes a body of practical knowledge built up over centuries of working the specific conditions of the Vastos plain. Communities that have lost parts of this knowledge through displacement or administrative disruption have consistently found that it takes generations to rebuild.

The social organization is organized around the herd rather than the fixed settlement. Communities have permanent winter quarters, typically in the more sheltered terrain at the plain's margins, but their effective authority is over the grazing range rather than over a defined territory. The concept of "territory" as an administrative boundary is a concept imported by outside powers; the Vastos communities' own land understanding is relational — who has the right to use which routes, at which seasons, in which sequence with which other herds. This relational understanding is specific, intricate, and does not map cleanly onto the boundary-based administrative systems that either Pyros or Ambron prefers to work with. Administrative powers have tried to translate it into boundary maps. The translations have consistently produced disputes that the Vastos communities resolve by reverting to their own system when the administering power is not watching.

## Administrative History

The documented history of Vastos administration is substantially a record of the same process at different historical moments. A power strong enough to project force into the upland plain establishes administrative presence, usually by making arrangements with the communities that control the main river crossings. An initial period of apparent success follows: goods flow, taxes are nominally collected, the administrative vocabulary of the holding power enters local speech. Then the holding power's attention or resources shift; the garrison thins; the prefectural infrastructure begins to function more nominally than practically; and eventually the administrative presence contracts to a formal claim that few people on the Vastos plain take seriously.

The Elagosi periods have generally lasted longer than the Pyrosi periods, primarily because Ambron's administrative machinery is better calibrated for managing territories through commercial rather than military integration. When Ambronite governance in Vastos works — which it does, in good periods — it works by making Vastos communities part of a trade network rather than by attempting to transform their practices. Vastos longhorns moving to Elagosi lake-country markets; dried meat and heavy wool reaching Ambronite distribution systems; return goods in the form of grain, metal, and manufactured items that the plain does not produce. The Ambronite-period material culture in Vastos communities is more visible archaeologically than the Pyrosi-period material culture, because the Ambronite periods produced a more sustained exchange. The language record is similar: the administrative Elagosi loanwords in Vastos Mittoli are numerous and deeply embedded, where the Pyrosi ceremonial vocabulary is present but remains recognizably borrowed rather than integrated.

## The People's View

The Vastos communities' own account of their history is organized around the herd, the route, and the long-run rather than around which power administered them in which century. This is not indifference to history. It is a different framework for which history matters. The Vastos oral tradition preserves the names of the great droughts, the years when the river failed to flood and the watering points dried, the particularly bad winters that killed entire herd-lines, the disputes over the river crossings that required community-wide mobilization. The names of Pyrosi chieftains who claimed suzerainty and Ambronite prefects who imposed quotas appear in the oral record as roughly equivalent to the names of the bad droughts: things that happened, that required management, that eventually passed.

The Vastos self-description, where one can be extracted from communities generally averse to self-description as a genre, is pastoral in its most fundamental orientation. "We follow the grass" is a phrase that appears in multiple recorded interactions, not as a metaphor but as a statement of organizing principle. The grass moves seasonally in its quality and abundance; the communities follow. The political administrations move in their own cycles; the communities accommodate them as they accommodate seasonal variation and return to the underlying practice when conditions permit.

This pragmatism is not without limits. The specific cases where Vastos communities have offered organized resistance — sporadic in the record but consistent in pattern — are cases where administrative demands would have required abandoning the seasonal routes. The river crossing controls, the mandatory settlement programs, the attempts to convert herd-based communities into fixed-location tax subjects: these have consistently produced the kind of resistance that pastoral communities produce when the alternative is losing the practice that makes the land work.

## Economy and Trade

Vastos's primary export is its livestock and the products that derive from them: live cattle for slaughter and breeding stock, preserved meat for markets that cannot be reached with live animals, heavy-grade wool for outer-layer textile production, and herd-management knowledge that occasionally travels in the form of Vastos practitioners hired as range consultants by lowland communities trying to establish upland grazing operations.

The secondary economy is transit-based. The routes that cross Vastos between the Pyros territories and the lake country must cross the Vastos plain, and the communities that control the reliable crossing points and the established watering infrastructure have always collected something for that service — formally when they are under administrative authority that requires formal collection, informally otherwise. The transit economy is modest compared to Yunethre's toll income but it is consistent, and the communities positioned on the main east-west routes maintain a commercial character that the central-plain communities, more purely pastoral, do not share.

What Vastos imports is grain and manufactured goods: the grain that cold upland conditions make difficult to grow reliably, and the metalwork, textiles, and other craft products that a pastoral economy without significant craft specialization requires. The direction of import trade has shifted with the administrative periods — more southward-grain-oriented in the Pyrosi periods when trade relationships pointed west and south, more lake-country-oriented in the Elagosi periods. In the intervals between major administrative presences, both directions operate, and the Vastos merchants who manage this trade maintain relationships with both spheres that are, characteristically, commercial rather than affiliative.
