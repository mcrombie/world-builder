# Gorgi Mountains

The **Gorgi Mountains** form the northern barrier of Azhora's interior — a high, cold, continuously glaciated range that separates the temperate and subarctic interior from the narrow **Witherst coast** on its northern face and the **Eshtor Plateau** above. They are not the highest range on the continent; the Oremindi exceed them in peak elevation and in the extreme of their glaciation. But they are among the most continuous, and their geography creates the sharpest climate discontinuity on Azhora — sharper than the Oremindi's, because the Oremindi span such longitude that their effects vary; the Gorgi presents a single wall whose logic is consistent along its entire length. North of the Gorgi is a different world. The difference is not gradual.

The Witherst people, whose coast lies on the northern face, have approximately eight weeks per year when the Gorgi passes are reliably crossable. This is not a planning constraint in the way that a seasonal port closure or a flood plain's inundation cycle is a planning constraint — something noted and accommodated. It is the structural condition of Witherst existence. The calendar of Witherst life, its trade cycle, its social schedule, its understanding of the rest of Azhora — all of these are organized around the eight weeks when the passes open and the forty-four weeks when they do not. The people on the other side of the Gorgi exist, for the Witherst, in a period of intense eight-week contact and then in a category of known-but-inaccessible for the remaining ten months. This is not experienced as isolation, because the Witherst do not compare themselves to people who have constant access. It is simply the shape of the year.

## Geography of the Three Faces

The designations **North Gorgi**, **East Gorgi**, and **West Gorgi** are administrative and navigational conventions from the southern side of the range. The Academy's geographic records use them. The trade authorities of the Lond plateau and the Henborth pass-keepers use them. They reflect how the range's three sections are distinguished from below: the central mass, the eastern terminus where the range descends toward the northeastern highlands, and the western terminus where it descends toward the coast.

The Witherst do not use these designations. Their orientation is from the northern face, and from the northern face the range is not divided into named sections — it is divided into passes and faces, specific named locations with specific known characters. A Witherst guide does not say they are crossing the North Gorgi; they say they are crossing the **Pelleth** or the **Amvord** or whichever specific pass is being discussed, and the choice of pass is determined by the specific conditions that week, not by the section designation. The Witherst naming system is denser in information than the southern administrative system, covers a smaller portion of the range in detail (the northern faces of the specific passes they use), and is essentially inaccessible to southern observers because it is in the Witherst language and because it references terrain features on the northern face that southern-trained travelers would not be able to locate.

The **North Gorgi** — the central designation — encompasses the main body of the range: the highest peaks, the most extensive glaciation, and the most reliable of the central passes. "Reliable" in this context means available for the full eight-week window in most years, as opposed to the shorter or more conditional availability of the eastern and western passes. The central passes are the ones the Witherst have used longest, know best, and return to as default. They are not gentle; they are simply the most manageable of the options the range provides.

The **East Gorgi** and **West Gorgi** designations cover the sections where the range's primary axis descends and breaks up. The eastern end descends toward the Baldro Mountains system; the western end descends toward the coast and the Bay of Lol approaches. In both cases, the descent does not mean the range becomes easy — it means the terrain becomes more broken, the passes more numerous and individually shorter but also more treacherous, and the total accumulated difficulty of the multiple smaller obstacles approaches and in bad conditions exceeds the difficulty of the central high passes. More options, each individually more dangerous than the central passes, is the accurate description.

## The Gorgi Window

The window opens from the Witherst perspective when the central passes become reliably passable — when the snowpack from winter has consolidated or ablated enough at the pass crests to allow movement without the avalanche risk that characterizes the winter pack, when the ice at the technical sections has retreated to positions that rope-and-ice technique can manage, and when the weather patterns of early summer have stabilized enough that the three-day crossings that the central passes require can be planned with reasonable confidence of not being trapped mid-crossing by a storm that would be merely uncomfortable in summer conditions and lethal in the exposure geometry of a high Gorgi crossing.

The eight-week duration is an average across years, not a fixed period. In good years the window extends to ten or eleven weeks. In poor years — cold spring, late consolidation, an unusual late-season storm system — it narrows to five or six weeks or, in exceptional years, fails to open in the usual sense: there is a window of two or three weeks of technical possibility that experienced crossing parties use but that is not the reliable passage that defines the normal window. These exceptional years are not uncommon enough to be disregarded. The Witherst have a specific category in their planning for years when the window fails, which includes the pre-positioned supplies and the alternative maritime routes that serve as the contingency.

From the southern side of the Gorgi, the window is observed as the period when Witherst trading parties appear at the Henborth and Lond approaches — suddenly, in the way of an annual event that is known to be coming but always arrives with the slightly surprising quality of something that was abstract and is now present. The Witherst trading parties that cross during the window carry the specific Witherst trade goods — **salt fish**, **sea ivory** from the Shore Elder coast, **amber** from the northern beach deposits — and they arrive with an intensity that the trading parties from other northern peoples do not match, because they know their commercial calendar is forty-four weeks of preparation compressed into eight weeks of transaction.

The window's social significance for the Witherst is not exhausted by the commercial exchange. The eight weeks are also when legal proceedings that require witnesses or parties from both sides of the range are conducted, when marriages are arranged across the range (less common than within the Witherst community, but documented), when information gathered in the southern interior reaches the northern coast and vice versa, when political adjustments that affect both sides of the range are negotiated or at minimum communicated. The social density of the window period — everything that requires cross-range presence happening in eight weeks — produces a specific quality of concentrated social life that outside observers who have witnessed it describe as unlike the rhythms of communities with year-round access to their neighbors.

## The Passes

### Central Passes

The central Gorgi passes are the range's primary crossings and the ones for which documentation is most detailed from both sides. There are three central passes in regular use — two in common use by experienced crossing parties, one in irregular use by parties with specific needs or knowledge — and the Academy has accounts of all three from guides who have crossed them and from the Witherst trading parties who have described them in the context of their own annual practice.

The passes share a character: high, cold, technically demanding at specific sections, exposed to weather for the full duration of the crossing in a way that lower passes in other ranges are not, and requiring three days at minimum for a crossing party moving with loads. The three-day requirement means overnight shelter at altitude is part of the planning for every crossing. The central passes have two standard overnight positions each — natural shelters improved by long generations of use, the improvements being simple and structural (windbreaks of placed stone, water collection surfaces cut into the rock, cache locations whose positions are known to experienced guides on both faces) rather than built infrastructure. These positions are maintained by the guides who use them; their maintenance is understood as mutual obligation among the people who have the passes as their professional territory.

The **Pelleth** — the most-used central pass, in the Academy's accounts — is the pass that Witherst guides describe as having the most consistent conditions. Its approach on the northern face is the longest of the three, requiring a full first day of ascent before the first overnight position; its descent on the southern face is more direct and faster. The asymmetry means that crossing parties from the south reach the first overnight position earlier in the day with more energy than crossing parties from the north, and the tactical advice from guides who know both sides is to start from the south when the weather is uncertain.

### Eastern and Western Passes

The eastern and western passes are the options that the broken terrain at the range's termini offers: more numerous, shorter individually, collectively more complex. A guide who knows the East Gorgi passes knows a dozen routes of varying difficulty, none of which is individually as demanding as the Pelleth but all of which require connecting into each other in sequences that depend on specific conditions — which section is consolidated this week, which is showing the particular ice formation that makes it treacherous rather than merely difficult, which is accessible from the specific approach the crossing party is using.

The western passes are the most documented from the southern side because the **Bay of Lol** maritime trade has historically included overland components through the western Gorgi approaches; the western pass guides are the professional class most visible to the northern Azhoran coastal record-keeping tradition. The eastern passes are less documented because the eastern approaches are less traveled — the Baldro Mountain country that the eastern Gorgi descends toward is itself complex terrain and the incentive for crossing the eastern Gorgi is lower than for crossing the central or western sections.

The pass guides — both faces — are the people whose knowledge gives them authority over the crossing. This is not a formal authority in the sense of an administrative appointment. It is the authority of indispensability: a crossing party that bypasses the guides on either face of the range does so at its own risk, and the risk has been demonstrated at sufficient historical frequency that bypassing the guides is understood as incompetent rather than independent. The guides on both faces know each other, coordinate across the range during the window, and maintain the passes as a shared professional commons that is not owned by any authority but is sustained by the guides' collective interest in its condition.

## The Northern Face and Witherst Perspective

The Gorgi's northern face is the Witherst world. It is where the range's character is most legible in the way that the face of something is more legible than its back — the northern face presents the range's ice and rock to the cold northern air without the temperate moderation that the southern face receives, and the result is glaciation more extensive than the southern face, ice more permanent at lower elevations, the aesthetic vocabulary of extreme cold applied to every surface.

The Witherst orientation toward the range is not the orientation of people who see it as a barrier. It is the orientation of people who live against it — who have built their coast communities at its northern foot, who use its geography for their own purposes, and who understand its properties with the intimacy that comes from generations of crossing it when it allows crossing and working around it when it does not. The Gorgi range, from the Witherst perspective, is simply the south wall of the world — massive, permanent, seasonal in its openings, and entirely known on its own face.

The **White Hills** — the specific Witherst term for the northern glaciated face of the range — appears in Witherst oral tradition as the feature that gives their coast its weather and its character. The wind that descends from the White Hills in winter is the specific cold that the Witherst coastal communities are built to withstand; the melt from the White Hills in summer is the specific seasonal flooding of the coastal watercourses that the Witherst build their settlements above; the ice of the White Hills is the source of the glacial meltwater that produces the **amber coast** deposits, the material that ends up in southern Azhoran markets and drives southern interest in the Gorgi crossing more than any other single Witherst trade good.

## Geology and Ice

The Gorgi is a young range in the geological sense — still rising in the specific way that ranges with active deep geology rise, by fractions that are not perceptible in a human lifetime but that accumulate over the timescales at which the range's current form becomes legible. The difference between young-range and old-range geology is visible at the surface: the Gorgi has the sharp ridgelines and steep faces of rock that has not had time to be fully worked by the frost and water cycles that round old mountains. The **Lotharn Mountains** to the south are old mountains; the comparison is legible without specialist knowledge. The Gorgi looks like it arrived recently, in geological terms. It looks difficult. It is difficult.

The glaciation is primarily on the north- and east-facing aspects, where the sunlight cannot reach the ice even in summer. The **Gorgi glaciers** are named individually in the Witherst system and collectively called the **icefields** in Northern Azhoran accounts that have tried to convey the aggregate impression. They are not small. The **central icefield** — the connected glacial system across the North Gorgi's highest section — is among the most extensive active glaciation on the continent outside the far north. It feeds the meltwater rivers on both faces of the range and is the source of the cold, milky, glacial-flour water that the Witherst coast communities use and that downstream communities on the southern face receive in the spring-summer melt pulse.

The glaciation is, by the Academy's most recent assessment, smaller than it was a generation ago. Whether this represents a secular trend or a temporary fluctuation in the pattern of years that have always included warm phases is the kind of question that the Academy has characterized as significant and difficult to settle from the current data. The guides who cross the central passes annually describe specific sections of the traditional routes as more exposed than they were in their training years, and the specific reference points that earlier generations described as reliable seasonal markers have shifted. The Witherst have a term for this — a word that the best Northern Mittoli translation renders as "the ice is thinking about leaving" — that may be descriptive, metaphorical, or both, and that they use without the register of alarm that outside observers might expect.

## Flora and Fauna

The Gorgi's vegetation is altitude-stratified in the way that all high ranges are stratified, and the northern face differs from the southern face in the predictable ways of a climate-discontinuity range: the treeline is higher on the southern face, the shrub communities are different, the alpine flora of the northern face reflects the sustained cold exposure that the southern face does not receive.

The **mountain goat** analogues of the Gorgi — the northern Azhoran equivalent of the Narcoshi goat, adapted to the specific rock character of this range — are present on both faces but more abundant on the southern face, where the season that makes the high rock available for grazing is longer. The Witherst hunt them on the northern face; they are a supplement to the Witherst coastal economy rather than a primary resource. The guides who cross the central passes treat them as a living indicator of the pass conditions — the specific positions the goats occupy on the rock faces near the passes give experienced guides information about the current state of the ice and snow that maps directly onto the pass condition assessment.

## The Southern Approach

From the south, approaching the Gorgi from the Lond plateau or through Henborth, the range announces itself at some distance. The central peaks are high enough to be visible from the flat Lond plain in clear weather, a white line on the northern horizon that the farmers know as the horizon's permanent feature. The approach itself — through the Henborth passes and the transitional country above — takes the traveler from the plateau's openness into the foothills and then the range proper over the course of two to three days of consistent upward movement.

The foothills south of the range are a specific landscape. They are not the range itself; they are the range's prologue — a series of steep-sided ridges and valleys that require navigating before the main crossings are reached, and that have their own weather and their own community of guides and settlement. The **southern pass villages** — the communities that position themselves at the last viable settlement location before the high pass approaches — are the southern equivalent of the Witherst northern face communities: people who have built their economic and social identity around the passes and their services, who know the approaches from below as thoroughly as the Witherst guides know the approaches from above, and whose interest in the range's continued accessibility is identical to that of their counterparts on the other face.

The southern guides and the Witherst guides meet at the pass crests during the window. The business conducted at those meetings — the exchange of information about conditions, the negotiation of specific crossing party arrangements, the maintenance of the shared pass infrastructure — is conducted in a trade register that has evolved specifically for this contact and that draws on Northern Mittoli grammar with Witherst vocabulary elements for the specific technical concepts that do not exist in Northern Mittoli because Northern Mittoli was not developed by people who cross the Gorgi range. It is a small, precise, functional language for a small, precise, functional relationship, and it works.
