# Eshtor Plateau

The **Eshtor Plateau** sits above the northern face of the Gorgi Mountains, on the far side of the mountain wall from the temperate Azhoran interior. It is high, cold, wind-exposed to a degree that the elevation alone does not fully explain, and documented almost exclusively from the testimony of people who have been there and chose to leave — which is everyone who has been there, because the plateau is not a place people remain in. The Witherst, whose coast lies below the Gorgi range's northern face, pass through the plateau's lower margins when the Gorgi window permits passage; the **Thalmagar**, whose range extends south from the cape into the approach country, move across it seasonally following the megafauna's routes; outside observers have provided accounts of both of these peoples in the plateau context, and neither account is what would be required to document the plateau's own character in any satisfying way.

The plateau is elevated substantially above the treeline. Nothing grows on it that requires cover from the wind, which leaves nothing growing on it that reaches above knee height, and nothing that reaches knee height is permanent. The wind off the northern ocean crosses the plateau without interruption. In summer this is a sustained, cold, directional force that shapes every decision about movement and camp; in winter it is something different in category, not merely more extreme but qualitatively changed by the temperature, so that the physical sensation — the Witherst accounts describe it in specific terms that the Northern Azhoran translators have found difficult to render, since the Witherst have specialized wind vocabulary that does not correspond to Northern Mittoli categories — is reportedly unlike what the word "cold" in standard usage prepares a person for.

## Geography

The Eshtor Plateau occupies the ground between the northern face of the Gorgi mountain wall and the transitional terrain that descends toward the Noth Hills and, beyond them, the tundra approach to Cape Thalmagar. It is elevated in the way that back-country terrain behind a major mountain range is often elevated — the range creates its own orographic effects, and the plateau on the northern face of the Gorgi sits in a climate system that the mountains have partially separated from the temperate influences that reach the southern side.

This climate discontinuity is the defining geographic fact of the Eshtor Plateau. The Gorgi range is not simply a terrain barrier; it is a climate boundary, and the plateau on its northern face exists in a colder, drier, more continuously wind-exposed system than the same latitude on the range's southern face would produce. The comparison is not with the climate of the southern Gorgi approaches — the plateau is dramatically colder than that. The comparison is with the Noth Hills country, which is the nearest broadly similar terrain, and the plateau is meaningfully colder than the hills despite sitting at roughly the same latitude. The mountains are responsible for this discrepancy, in the sense that they block the southward flow of the cold northern air elsewhere while the plateau itself sits exposed to it.

Snow cover on the Eshtor Plateau persists substantially longer than on the slopes below. The southern Gorgi slopes lose their snow cover in late spring; the plateau above the northern face holds snow into summer in sheltered areas and retains a hard base of compacted snow in the north-facing hollows through the warmest months in most years. The northern ocean, which might moderate the temperature the way it moderates the cape's climate, is too far and too obstructed by the terrain for its effect to significantly reach the plateau. The plateau gets the cold without the oceanic buffer.

The **plateau surface** itself is the kind of terrain that the word "plateau" technically describes — elevated, broadly flat by comparison with the mountains below it — but is not flat in the sense of being uniform or easy. The surface is interrupted by the **eshtor ridges**: low rock outcrops that run roughly east-west across the plateau in a pattern that suggests an ancient structural direction in the underlying geology, now exposed by the erosion that the wind and the frost cycles together produce on terrain this elevated and this cold. The ridges are not high — the tallest are perhaps fifteen meters above the plateau surface — but they are significant as windbreaks, and every account of the Thalmagar moving across the plateau notes the pattern of night camps placed behind the lee side of these ridges whenever the distance between ridge and destination makes it possible.

## The Wind and Weather

The plateau's wind is not a variable. It is a constant to which conditions are measured as deviations. The Witherst, who pass through the plateau margins during the Gorgi window and who have the most extensive wind vocabulary of any northern Azhoran people, have terms for the plateau's specific wind character that the nearest Northern Mittoli translation renders as something like "the wind that has come from far" — a term distinguishing it from the local wind generated by terrain features and coastal interaction, which is the wind the Witherst most commonly describe. The plateau wind is the northern ocean's wind, unmodified by anything between the water and the ground.

The weather system that the Gorgi range creates is visible in its effects on the plateau. Storms that rise against the southern face of the mountains build moisture against the range and then drop it there; the northern face and the plateau above it sit in the rain shadow in the sense of receiving less precipitation, but "rain shadow" in the sense of dry and warm is not applicable here. The reduced precipitation falls at lower temperatures and stays where it falls, and the wind distributes it into the ridge-and-hollow geography of the plateau in a pattern that creates the deep drifts behind the eshtor ridges that the Thalmagar use for orientation as much as for shelter — the drift geometry tells the direction of the last major wind, the density of the snow tells how recent, and both together inform the experienced traveler's assessment of the plateau's condition in a way that the Witherst and Thalmagar describe as reading and that outside observers have found genuinely difficult to replicate.

Summer on the plateau is brief and qualified. The warmth is real — the plateau sees temperatures above freezing for perhaps four months, and during the warmest weeks the exposed rock surfaces absorb enough heat that small weather systems develop above them, producing the cumulus buildups that the plateau's open horizon shows in every direction to the traveler standing at its center. These buildups produce afternoon rainfall that is the plateau's most significant summer precipitation and that fills the hollows behind the ridges with water that is, for the duration of the warm period, the only reliable fresh water on the surface. After the rains the plateau smells of the specific wet-rock and cold-moss combination that people who have been there consistently mention and that is, apparently, distinctive enough to be recalled accurately years later.

## Megafauna Seasonal Routes

The **Long-Back** uses the Eshtor Plateau as transitional movement ground in a seasonal pattern that the Thalmagar track with the attention that people give to information whose accuracy has direct consequences. The Long-Back's range on the Thalmagar cape is well-documented in the context of the cape's own ecology; what is less documented outside the Thalmagar's own knowledge systems is the extent to which the Long-Back moves beyond the cape into the surrounding territory, and the plateau is part of this extended range.

The Long-Back's movement across the plateau is not random. The animal follows a route system defined by the distribution of the specific canopy tree species it prefers — on the plateau, this means the sparse trees that grow in sheltered positions on the south-facing lee sides of the eshtor ridges, in the same hollows that accumulate the summer water. These trees are at the extreme upper margin of their climate range; they are small, compressed, and slow-growing, and they occur at intervals that determine the pattern of the Long-Back's movement across the plateau. The animal moves from one grove to the next in a pattern that the Thalmagar have traced and named and that constitutes the most detailed outside account of the plateau's geography available — not because it was assembled with the plateau's geography as the object of study, but because understanding the Long-Back's movements required understanding what the Long-Back was moving between.

The **Cape Palmant** does not regularly use the plateau in the same way. The Palmant's seasonal movements cross the plateau margin — the lower edges, where the terrain begins to slope toward the Noth Hills — but the plateau's open, wind-exposed character is not the Palmant's preferred terrain. The herds use the plateau edges as transition zones: they cross them rather than using them as residence ground. The Thalmagar note the distinction. The Long-Back, which has been browsing the eshtor ridge tree stands for long enough to have shaped their distribution (the ridge trees are most dense on the ridges the Long-Back habitually visits, an effect that has not been established as causal but is consistent enough to be suggestive), has an apparently older relationship with the plateau than the Palmant's transitional use implies.

## The Thalmagar View

The Thalmagar do not offer accounts of the Eshtor Plateau to outside observers in the way that outside observers frame requests for accounts. The plateau is part of their range, which means it is known the way range is known — in specifics that do not abstract well into general descriptions, and in a context of practical knowledge whose value is not enhanced by transmission to people who will not be doing anything with it.

What has reached outside documentation through the trading contacts that the Witherst maintain with Northern Azhoran coast communities, and through the second-hand accounts of northern Azhoran sailors who have spoken with Thalmagar trading contacts at the cape, is a version of the Thalmagar plateau knowledge that is summary and practical in character. The plateau is crossable. The eshtor ridges are the landmarks. The Long-Back's routes are the secondary landmarks. The water is in the hollow-bases in summer and requires knowing the specific hollows in winter, when it is locked. The plateau is not a place to be caught in the open in the severe-cold season.

What the plateau means to the Thalmagar — beyond the practical — is not established. Whether the plateau has a presence in their oral tradition in the way that the cape's megafauna and the Outer Deep and the Oremindi appear in the fragments of their tradition that have been transmitted through the trading contact, is not known. The Thalmagar do not appear to have been asked about the plateau specifically in a context where the question was understood; the records of trading contacts focus on the cape and the megafauna and the sea products, and the plateau is the territory behind those things, which is where it sits in the Thalmagar's own accounting of what is important and in outside observers' accounting simultaneously.

## Approach and Access

The Eshtor Plateau is accessed from the south by crossing the Gorgi range, which means it is accessible for approximately eight weeks per year under normal conditions. The Gorgi central passes — the ones the Witherst use for their annual window of contact with the southern interior — emerge on their northern faces at the plateau's lower margin. The Witherst, who cross these passes in both directions during the window, move across the plateau on their way to the northern coast below, and it is in this transit capacity that most of the accounts of the plateau from identifiable sources have been generated.

The Witherst do not linger on the plateau. They have their own coast, their own world, their own calendar, and the plateau is the crossing between those things and the south, not a destination in itself. What the Witherst accounts of the plateau contain is the observation of a people passing through a specific terrain with professional attention to weather and footing and the logistic requirements of the crossing, not the contemplative description of a people who have stopped and looked. The wind vocabulary that the Witherst deploy in plateau descriptions is the most precise documentation of the plateau's weather character available from outside the Thalmagar tradition.

From the north, the plateau is accessed from the cape and the Noth Hills territory — the Thalmagar's approach — across terrain that has no fixed infrastructure. No passes, in the formal sense that the Gorgi passes are passes; the transition from hill country to plateau is gradual enough that the boundary is practical rather than geographic. The people who know the northern approach know it by movement, not by a pass that marks the change.

The absence of documentation for the Eshtor Plateau relative to the attention the Gorgi Mountains themselves receive is a straightforward consequence of access. Researchers and merchants and administrators from the southern interior see the Gorgi range from its southern face, know the passes in the context of the annual window, and understand the northern side of the range as the place the Witherst come from and return to. What sits above the Witherst coast — the plateau between the mountain wall and the tundra — is beyond the scope of what the window justifies reaching for. The Thalmagar have not chosen to explain it in detail. The documentation gap reflects these facts precisely.
