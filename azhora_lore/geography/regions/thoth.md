# Thoth

The Thoth country is cold scrubland. This description is accurate and insufficient in the specific way that accurate descriptions of overlooked places tend to be insufficient — it describes the surface without describing the character, names the vegetation without naming the people, and it is used, generally, by people approaching the Thoth from the outside rather than by anyone who has organized their life in relation to it. The territory spreads across the gradients between the foothills of the northern ranges to the south, the open tundra of the **Cape Thalmagar** approaches to the north, and the **Thalmagar megafauna range** at the center — and its three named sub-regions, **Central Thoth**, **North Thoth**, and **South Thoth**, reflect these gradients in the orientation of whoever applied the designations. The people who actually live in the Thoth territory do not use these divisions. Their own orientation is by specific terrain features, water sources, and the specific routes that the megafauna use and that people have followed long enough to know.

The name appears consistently in the oldest layers of northern Azhoran geographic knowledge and has not been successfully attributed to any known language family. It is not Northern Mittoli in form; it is not the pre-Mittoli substrate whose phonological profile appears in Henborth, Lond, and the Canerd-related names. It does not correspond to any Witherst or Thalmagar root that outside scholars have been able to examine. The Academy's most recent assessment is that the name is either the oldest surviving toponym in the northwest, predating all known substrate layers and therefore beyond analysis, or is from a language so thoroughly unattested that no reconstruction is possible. Both possibilities point to the same conclusion: the name is very old, and what it means has not survived the people who first applied it.

## Geography and the Three Sub-Regions

The Thoth territory occupies a roughly triangular area in the western northern approaches to Cape Thalmagar. Its southern apex is in the foothills where the terrain that connects to the Lond-Henborth country begins to open out into the wider western scrubland; its northern extent reaches toward the tundra edge where the scrub thins into open ground. The eastern margin is where the foothills of the Noth Hills system intrude on the scrubland character; the western margin is where the terrain reaches the coast and the coastal character asserts itself, and which is Witherst country rather than Thoth country.

**South Thoth** is the sub-region where the foothills that connect to the southern approaches are most present — the ground that is not yet the open scrub but is not the hills either, where streams from the northern ranges produce more-defined watercourses and the ground is broken enough to provide the orientation features that pure scrubland does not offer. South Thoth is where the Lond-oriented traffic that crosses through the Thoth territory enters it, and where the communities that function as intermediaries between the southern farming world and the Thoth's own peoples position themselves, when they position themselves relative to southern traffic at all.

**Central Thoth** is the open scrubland proper — the territory where the character the outside world associates with Thoth is most fully expressed. Windswept, cold, the vegetation a low carpet of **cold-adapted heather**, **northern sedge**, and the specific **Thoth scrub-willow** that is the most prominent plant in this landscape: a willow that grows to knee height at most, its branches compressed by the wind into the aerodynamic lateral spread that dominates the visual character of the Central Thoth in every direction at ground level. Central Thoth is also where the Thalmagar megafauna range overlaps most extensively with the Thoth territory — where the Long-Back and the Cape Palmant use the scrubland in their seasonal movements in patterns that the Thalmagar track and that the Thoth peoples have been living alongside for as long as their communities have existed in this territory.

**North Thoth** is the sub-region where the scrubland grades into the tundra approach. The distinction between scrubland and tundra is not sharp; it is the kind of gradual change that is legible in retrospect, when the traveler notices that the scrub-willow has been getting shorter and sparser for some distance and that the heather is no longer covering the ground continuously and that the open patches between vegetation are wider than they were. North Thoth is where the Thalmagar seasonal movement to and from the south passes through the Thoth territory, and where the interaction between the Thalmagar and the Thoth peoples — not intensive, not frequent, but existing — takes place most often.

## The Name

The name deserves more attention than its brevity suggests. A place name that cannot be analyzed, cannot be attributed to any surviving language, and appears in the oldest geographic records without explanation or origin story is either an accident of the documentary record or a genuine antiquity — a word that survived because the geography it named was continuous enough to require a name through all the linguistic changes that have transformed the surrounding vocabulary.

The Thalmagar, who know the territory the name covers and whose own system of reference covers the same ground in a different vocabulary, do not use this name, as noted. This is consistent with the pattern of names that were applied from outside — by whoever was doing the earliest northern Azhoran geographical record-keeping, which was not the Thalmagar and was not the people who live in the Thoth territory, neither of whom have needed an outside name for ground they know in their own terms. The outside name stuck because the people who needed a name for this territory in a transmissible geographic record were the people approaching from the south: the Lond-country peoples and the trading networks that connected the north to the interior. For them, "Thoth" is the western approach country to the cape. For the people who live in it, it is home, and home does not need a name imposed from outside.

The one analysis that has been seriously proposed — that the name shares phonetic elements with archaic forms in a very early layer of Grassic, the language of the Plains peoples — has been noted by two independent scholars and has not been developed into anything more than a note. Whether a connection to the Grassic family is possible would require a more complete early Grassic vocabulary than currently exists. The Academy has not prioritized this line of investigation, which may be a deficit in the Academy's priorities rather than evidence against the connection.

## The Scrubland Ecology

The Thoth scrubland is a cold-climate ecosystem organized around the specific constraints of the territory: short growing season, persistent wind, thin rocky soil in the northern and central sections and slightly better drained peaty soil in the south, frost return in early autumn and frost persistence late into spring. The vegetation that survives these conditions is not simple, though it is low.

The **Thoth scrub-willow** dominates visually but is not the most ecologically significant plant in the territory. The heather communities of Central Thoth — dense mats of interspecific heather in six or seven varieties that the Thoth peoples distinguish and use differently — provide the ground-layer structure that retains moisture, accumulates organic matter, and provides the food base for the grazing animals that use the scrubland. The heather vocabulary of the Thoth communities is the most specific plant vocabulary in their speech, reflecting the degree to which heather management and heather reading are practical skills with immediate consequences.

The **northern bog sedge** concentrates in the hollows and along the drainage lines, where the water table is accessible through a longer season than the exposed ridges allow. The sedge communities are where the Thoth people harvest the specific basket and mat material that is one of the territory's export goods — coarse, durable, cold-climate weaving material that does not have a southern equivalent because the plants that produce it do not grow at lower latitudes in the same quality. The sedge hollows are also where the megafauna drink, which means the Thoth peoples know which hollows the large animals use, and organize their own use of those hollows accordingly.

The **Thoth moss** is a collective name that the Academy has used for the complex of bryophytes that cover every surface in the wetter sections of the scrubland. The Thoth peoples have specific names for specific mosses and specific uses for them — wound treatment, insulation, water filtration, fire-starting material (the dried-moss technique is specific to the northern peoples and produces a specific quality of fire appropriate to cold-weather starting conditions). The moss vocabulary of the Thoth dialects is another dense specialized register, less documented than the heather vocabulary but more practically broad in its applications.

## Peoples of the Thoth

The Thoth territory is not continuously inhabited in the way that the Lond plateau or even Henborth is. There are communities here — people whose adaptation to the cold-scrubland ecology is specific and old — but they are small, widely spaced, and not distributed in the way that agricultural communities distribute themselves across productive land. They are located where the specific combination of requirements is met: water, shelter from the dominant wind direction, proximity to the resources that their particular community harvests or herds, and a position that gives enough warning of the megafauna movements that the community's safety protocols are workable.

These communities have been overlooked in the documentation that concentrates on the more legible northern peoples — the Lond farmers with their grain economy and their Stonefist politics, the Thalmagar with their megafauna cohabitation and their cape isolation, the Witherst with their dramatic window-of-contact cycle. The Thoth peoples present neither the administrative legibility of the Lond farming economy nor the dramatic megafauna story of the Thalmagar. They present a life adapted to cold scrubland, which is harder to make interesting to outside observers, and their smallness of number and dispersal across difficult terrain have historically discouraged the extended contact that documentation requires.

What is known from the accounts that do exist: the Thoth communities are distinct from both the Lond farming culture and the Thalmagar coastal tradition, and they are conscious of this distinction. They are not failed farmers who could not make the plateau work; they are scrubland peoples who have organized their economy and their knowledge around the specific resources the scrubland offers, and who do not regard the plateau's grain economy as the superior version of life that the Lond farmers might assume it represents. The specific herding that the southern Thoth communities practice — **fell-sheep** of a northern breed distinct from the Endevorfolk's fell-sheep, smaller, adapted to the specific Thoth scrub-grazing — is not a precursor to plateau grain farming. It is a different thing.

The **northern Thoth communities** are the hardest to document and the least engaged with the southern economy. They have the most regular awareness of the Thalmagar — proximity creates contact — and the most developed relationship with the megafauna-adjacent landscape that North Thoth represents. Whether they are aware of themselves as northern people of the Thoth or simply as people of specific communities in specific locations is not established. The category "Thoth peoples" is an outside analytical frame applied to people who may not use it for themselves.

## The Thalmagar Overlap

The Thalmagar range extends into the Thoth territory in its eastern margin, and this overlap is not incidental. The Thalmagar's knowledge of their range is organized around the megafauna whose movements define the cape's practical geography; the megafauna whose movements cross into the Thoth — primarily the Long-Back and the Cape Palmant — bring the Thalmagar's range into the Thoth's territory as a functional consequence. The Thalmagar are in the Thoth when the large animals are in the Thoth, because knowing where the large animals are requires being where they are.

The Thalmagar do not use the name "Thoth" for this territory — their spatial vocabulary for the eastern margin of their range references specific terrain features, specific seasonal water sources, specific ridge lines that the Long-Back uses as travel corridors. These references have been described to outside observers in second-hand accounts and consistently fail to translate into the cartographic form that outside observers want, because they are references in a vocabulary designed for navigation and not description.

The relationship between the Thalmagar and the Thoth peoples is not the relationship between a dominant people and a marginal one. Both are small, both are adapted to the same difficult northern territory, and both have developed their knowledge of the land in parallel. The Thalmagar's knowledge of the megafauna is the most complete; the Thoth peoples' knowledge of the scrubland ecology is more detailed in the small-scale resource dimensions that the Thalmagar, as a coastal people, have less reason to develop. These knowledge systems are not formally exchanged; the contact that the overlap produces is too infrequent and too practically focused for systematic knowledge exchange. But the communities on both sides of the overlap are aware of each other and have been for long enough that the awareness is a background condition rather than a notable event.

The specific question of whether the Thoth peoples and the Thalmagar share a deep historical connection — whether the dispersed scrubland communities represent a population that was once more connected to the cape's peoples, separated by the megafauna's territorial development or by historical circumstances that are not documented — has not been investigated from outside, and neither the Thalmagar nor the Thoth peoples have offered an account of this relationship that outside observers have received.

## Seasonal Movement

The Thoth is a seasonal landscape in the sense that all cold-climate ecosystems are seasonal — what the territory offers and what can be done with it changes substantially across the year — but the seasonal movement it supports is not only the movement of the Thoth peoples themselves. It is also the seasonal passage of peoples from outside the territory: the Thalmagar in their south-ward movements during cape harshness, the northern communities from the Noth Hills and Henborth country using the western approach routes to and from the cape, and occasionally the Lond-oriented traders who find the western approach to the Thalmagar contact a more viable route in specific seasonal conditions than the main Lond-Henborth axis.

The Thoth peoples' own seasonal movement is organized around the heather harvest, the sedge hollows, and the fell-sheep's range, all of which shift with the season in patterns that require the communities to move with them or to coordinate their use across the territory's extent. This movement is not random migration; it is a systematic seasonal pattern that the communities maintain as the structural basis of their economy. The winter concentration — when the communities pull back to their most sheltered positions and live on the stored summer harvest — is the season when the Thoth is, to an outside observer, apparently empty of people. It is not empty. The people are in the places they go when the season requires it, and those places are known to the people who live there and not obvious to anyone else.

The seasonal passage of outside peoples through the Thoth does not significantly disrupt the communities' own seasonal patterns, because the passing peoples use the routes that the terrain defines — the drainage lines, the specific low-point crossings — and the communities have organized their seasonal positions partly in relation to these routes, in the way that communities that exist in passage territory organize themselves: close enough to the traffic to benefit from it, not so close that the traffic and the community's needs conflict.
