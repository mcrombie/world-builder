# Dinelv Highlands

Above the city of Dinelv, the Dinova Peninsula rises from its eastern coastal strip into an arid upland plateau that extends inland toward the Meroshe's margins and provides the kingdom's elevation. The distinction between the city and the highlands above it is not merely topographic. Dinelv is a administrative and commercial city oriented toward the sea and the road, populated by officials and merchants whose frame of reference is the Iberos trade and the caravan economy. The highlands above it are populated by people who have been on that plateau longer than the administrative class has been in the city below, and who regard the city's business with a detachment that is not hostility so much as perspective. They were here before the current arrangement and they expect to remain after it. The Maroshi court does not fully administer the highlands; it surveys them, garrisons their strategic points, and contracts their products. This is operationally adequate. Whether it constitutes governance depends on what one means by the term.

---

## Geography

The **Dinelv Plateau** — the name used in the Route Registry's topographic records, though the highland communities use older local names for their specific areas that do not correspond to the administrative term — rises sharply from the coastal strip through a series of stepped escarpments. The ascent from the city takes a full day on the standard road and longer on the secondary tracks. The escarpment face is the most dramatic terrain on the eastern peninsula: exposed sedimentary rock cut by seasonal water channels, with the older geological layers visible in the cliff faces as horizontal bands of different character. The lower bands are the warm-toned desert stone that the Dinelv construction trade prizes; the upper bands shift to a harder, darker stone with less commercial appeal but better structural properties for load-bearing applications.

At the top of the escarpments, the plateau opens into a rolling upland that extends twenty to forty miles inland before the terrain descends again toward the Meroshe interior. The surface is arid — rainfall at the plateau elevation is somewhat higher than on the coast directly below, but not substantially, and the thin soils and rocky substrate do not retain what moisture falls. The vegetation is scrub: low, spaced, adapted to the dryness, with deeper-rooted plants occupying the water-concentration points that only become visible in wet years when they green faster than the surrounding ground.

The plateau is not flat. A series of ridge systems crosses it from roughly north to south, aligned with the peninsula's long axis. These ridges create the passes that matter to the Route Registry: the **northern plateau pass** on the main caravan road, the **middle saddle** used by lighter traffic and express riders, and several smaller routes that the Registry characterizes as "local use" but that the highland communities use for movements the Registry does not record. The garrison settlements are located at the northern plateau pass and the middle saddle. The smaller routes have no garrison presence.

Stone is the plateau's primary export to the city. The lower escarpment quarries supply the warm-toned construction stone that Dinelv's public buildings use for their facades. The upper plateau's harder stone goes to road construction and to the structural elements of harbor works. The quarrying operations are among the few plateau economic activities that the Maroshi court has organized directly, because stone supply to the city is a court priority and because the quarries' fixed location makes them administrable in a way that pastoral communities are not.

## The Highland Population

The plateau population is not primarily urban and is not primarily oriented toward Dinelv. The largest concentrations of highland settlement are the pastoral communities that follow seasonal movement patterns across the plateau — higher and further inland in the cooler wet months, lower and closer to the escarpment in the dry summer when the water sources at higher elevation fail. These communities are Moreshi-speaking, but their dialect sits closer to the canyon communities' speech than to the Coastal Trade Moreshi of the city. The [r]/[ʀ] distinction that the canyon traditions maintain is preserved in the highland dialect; the specific lexical contrasts that the distinction marks in canyon Moreshi are present in the highland speech, though with different distribution. Coastal Maroshi scholars who have studied the highland dialect classify it as transitional between canyon and coastal registers, which is accurate as a description and tells one nothing about how the highland speakers understand their own speech.

The highland communities maintain their own genealogical records, separate from the court's population registers. Court population registers are a recurring administrative priority at Dinelv, since accurate population figures affect the calculation of tribute assessments and conscription pools. The highland communities provide counts when asked, and the counts are never obviously false and never complete. The discrepancy between the census figures and the actual population is not known, because the purpose of the census is to generate a figure that the court can use administratively, and a figure the court can use is what the communities provide. Whether the undercounting is deliberate is a question the registry officials prefer not to press.

The mining communities are more fixed in location and therefore more visible to the court. The ridge-exposure mines are worked by communities that have lived adjacent to specific mineral outcrops for generations; their knowledge of the specific deposits — where the best stone runs, where it thins, where fault structures make extraction dangerous — is held in oral tradition that no Dinelv mining official has successfully elicited in full. The court's mining contractors work the most accessible deposits under the supervision of highland families who have agreed to cooperate. The deeper knowledge of the ridge exposures remains with the families.

Garrison settlements are a third population type, qualitatively different from the others. The garrisons at the plateau passes are established Maroshi military communities with their own supply lines to the city, their own administrative structure, and their own social world that overlaps minimally with the highland communities around them. The garrison populations do not learn the highland dialect. They communicate with the surrounding communities through the commercial transactions of supply — the pastoral communities sell the garrisons food at rates that both sides understand are not market rates — and through the occasional formal interaction when a highland community requires the court's authority for a specific purpose. The garrison commanders file regular reports on route security. Their reports on the highland communities are of limited analytical value because the commanders do not have the access to produce better ones.

## Maroshi Administration

The Maroshi court administers the Dinelv Highlands through three instruments: the garrison presence, the tribute system, and the quarrying contracts. Of these, the quarrying contracts are the most effective because they are the most specific: a contract for a defined quantity of a defined stone type, delivered to the city escarpment road at a defined schedule, at an agreed price. The terms are concrete enough to be enforceable, and both sides have reason to honor them. The pastoral tribute is less effective, because assessing what the pastoral communities owe requires knowledge of herd sizes that the court does not possess and the communities do not volunteer. The tribute is collected at a rate that all parties understand is approximate, which means it is collected at a rate that the communities find acceptable rather than a rate that the court has calculated from actual assessment.

Surveillance is the court's preferred instrument for the things it cannot directly administer. **The Plateau Watch** — the rider network that maintains communication between the garrison posts and reports on route conditions — is the most consistently funded element of the highland administration. The court needs to know three things: whether the main passes are open, whether caravans are crossing without challenge, and whether anything is moving on the plateau in unusual volume or direction. The Watch provides this. What it does not provide, and what the court does not ask for directly, is information about the highland communities' internal politics, their relationships with the canyon communities to the interior, or the uses to which the smaller plateau routes that have no garrison are put.

The court's position is that the passes are secure and the routes are administered. This is true at the level of the main commercial arteries. Whether it is true of the plateau as a whole is a different question, and one that the Route Registry's formal records address only obliquely.

## The Passes

The **northern plateau pass** is the primary overland connection between Dinelv and the plateau interior, and through the plateau interior, to the caravan routes that cross the Meroshe. It is the widest and most regularly maintained of the plateau crossings, capable of accommodating the full caravan trains that are the basis of Marosh's commercial operation. The road through the pass has been maintained by the court since the early period of Maroshi expansion — the current road surface incorporates sections that the Route Registry dates by their construction technique to well before the current dynasty, which implies that the pass was in use and maintained under political arrangements the current court no longer knows about.

The garrison at the northern pass is the largest highland military establishment. Its function is as much commercial as military: the garrison operates the waystation that serves caravans on the pass road, maintains the water cisterns that make the ascent and descent navigable in summer, and enforces the tariff inspection that applies to goods coming up from the city or down from the plateau interior. The garrison commander is a senior Registry official as well as a military officer — the same appointment covers both functions, which reflects the court's understanding that the pass is primarily a commercial instrument.

The **middle saddle** is used by lighter traffic: express riders, small trading parties, and the livestock movements that the highland pastoral communities manage seasonally. It is not maintained to the standard of the northern pass and is not operable for full caravan trains. The garrison at the middle saddle is smaller and its function is primarily observational: it notes what crosses and reports to Dinelv. It does not collect tariffs on the smaller movements because the cost of doing so would exceed the revenue, and attempting it would redirect traffic to the smaller unmonitored routes.

## Economy

Stone and meat are the two significant highland products. Stone moves downhill to Dinelv on a regular commercial schedule and constitutes the plateau's primary contribution to the city's economy. Meat moves in a more complex pattern: the pastoral communities slaughter and preserve in the autumn when herd sizes are at their seasonal high, and the preserved products move to the city, to the garrisons, and to the caravan way-stations in quantities that vary with the year's grazing conditions.

What the court cannot easily tax is the pastoral economy's secondary products: the fiber from the highland breeds, the dairy processed into preserved forms, the highland crafts that move through the small routes to the canyon communities and to the trade networks that the canyon peoples maintain with their own sphere of exchange. These goods enter a parallel economy that the Route Registry acknowledges in general terms — the "interior trade" appears in the Registry's context documents as a known phenomenon — without tracking in the specific terms that would allow taxation. The volume of this interior trade is not known to the court. The highland communities' wealth, to the extent that it can be assessed, is consistently underestimated by Dinelv administrators who are counting only what passes through the monitored routes.

The highland population's material standard is generally adequate by the standards of arid-zone pastoral communities on Azhora: not prosperous in the urban commercial sense, but self-sufficient in ways that the city below is not. The city depends on food supply routes that the highlands partly control through the seasonal pass access. The highlands depend on the city's commercial demand for their stone and their surplus meat. The mutual dependency is not symmetric — the city has more commercial alternatives than the plateau — but it is real enough that the court's decisions about highland tribute rates are made with an eye on not disrupting the supply of construction materials that Dinelv requires for its continuous building program.

The specific economic relationship between the highland communities and the canyon peoples further interior is not documented by the court and not discussed by either set of communities with outside researchers. That it exists is evident from the movement patterns the Plateau Watch notes but does not follow. What it covers — whether purely commercial, whether also involving the older social and religious ties that the highland Moreshi dialect's shared features with canyon speech might imply — is not known.
