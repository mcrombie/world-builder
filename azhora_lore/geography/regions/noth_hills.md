# Noth Hills

The Noth Hills are old in a way that shows. Elsewhere in northern Azhora the landscape makes gestures toward drama — the Oremindi's sharp ridgelines, the Gorgi's glaciated faces, the Thalmagar cape's sudden cliffs. The Noth Hills do none of this. They are worn down, rounded, their edges softened by the long work of ice and the longer work of what the ice left behind when it receded: a sequence of gentle rises and shallow dells that shape movement across the upland without announcing themselves as obstacles. They are between **Nothwood** to the south and the open tundra that approaches **Cape Thalmagar** to the north, the middle country of the far northwest — not the forest, not the cape, not quite either. The geologist's record is legible on their surfaces in a way it is not where softer rock and soil cover the deep history. In the hill flanks and the outcrops and the valley floors, the hills are showing their age.

The name is pre-Mittoli substrate and does not reconstruct cleanly from any known root system, though "noth" appears in Forest Mittoli substrate vocabulary in a context associated with old highland terrain — the kind of word that names a quality of a place rather than a description of it. Whether the word is the same word in both contexts, or whether the correspondence is coincidental, the Caeras Valley Academy has not determined. The Thalmagar do not use this name for the hills; they have their own system, organized around specific features rather than regional designations, and the outside name sits over the landscape without touching how it is actually navigated.

## Geography

The Noth Hills occupy the transitional ground between the open tundra of the Thalmagar approaches and the northern edge of the continuous Azhoran forest cover. Their extent is roughly that of a long day's walk east to west and two days' walk north to south — not large in the way that the Oremindi or the Gorgi are large, but large enough that the traveler who enters them at one edge without reliable local knowledge cannot assume an exit at the other. The hills occupy their territory with a kind of quiet insistence. The ridgelines are not high, but they are constant, and the ground between them channels movement in ways that do not become apparent until the traveler has been channeled.

The geology of the Noth Hills is the record of what happened here before anything else. The basement rock is among the oldest exposed on the continent — ancient granites and metamorphics that the pre-Mittoli substrate vocabulary, if it names anything here, names in categories that suggest very long habitation of the concept, if not the place. The ice that covered this country in earlier ages found rock that resisted it differently at different points, and what resulted is the particular kind of upland relief that geologists describe as knock-and-lochan country: resistant outcrops standing above the general grade, softer rock hollowed between them into basins and channels, the whole surface scored and polished and fractured by the ice's passage.

**Glacial striations** are visible on every exposed rock surface of any size — parallel grooves, some shallow, some centimeters deep, all oriented in the same general southwest-to-northeast direction that records the ice's direction of movement across the landscape. They have not weathered to invisibility because the cold and dry climate of the northern upland does not produce the chemical weathering that would erase them; they are as legible now as when the ice left them. The **deposited erratics** — boulders carried here from far to the north and left when the ice retreated — are scattered across the hills without apparent pattern, sitting in the landscape as objects so obviously foreign to their setting that the brain reaches for an agent to explain their placement. There is no agent. The ice set them down where it ran out of energy to carry them, and there they remain: rocks from places further north than most travelers have gone, sitting in the Noth Hills like correspondence from a country that no longer sends letters.

The drainage of the Noth Hills is complex and locally specific. Water drains off the outcrops and into the basins between the knolls, accumulates in peat bogs in the wider hollows, and eventually finds its way south through the Nothwood and north toward the tundra in streams that are reliable in character and unreliable in specific course. The bogs between the hills are not the peat bogs of the lowland forest — they are thinner, colder, and more treacherous underfoot, because the permafrost beneath them means the frozen layer and the thawed layer above it are inconsistent in distribution, and a stretch of ground that holds weight in early summer will not hold it in late summer when the thaw has penetrated further.

## The Terrain

The hills' rounded profile is functionally deceptive. The upland traveler who plots a route by the general shape of the land — following the high ground, avoiding the low — finds that the hills cooperate up to a point and then do not. The higher ground between the rounded summits and the tundra to the north is often drier and faster to move across, but the hill crests also expose the traveler to the wind that rolls off the cape and its approach tundra with nothing to interrupt it above the hill profile. In summer this is an inconvenience; in the shoulder seasons it is a condition that has killed people who underestimated the rate at which their body heat was being taken.

Movement through the Noth Hills follows a logic that is not cartographic. The hill crests offer visibility and generally better footing; the valley floors between the hills offer shelter and water and the animal paths that have been maintained by the regular passage of megafauna over generations. Neither option is unconditionally better, and the choice between them shifts with season, weather, and destination in a way that the Thalmagar understand as integrated knowledge and that outside travelers tend to encounter as a series of bad surprises.

The hill crests are where the outcrops concentrate. The **Noth ridge outcrops** — the largest formations on the hill system — stand above the rounded summits like teeth above gums, vertically jointed in the way of ancient granites that contracted as they cooled. They are not large by mountainous standards, but they provide the Noth Hills with what the hills would otherwise lack: fixed, visible landmarks against a skyline that is otherwise featureless from below. The Thalmagar use the outcrops as reference points in a spatial vocabulary for the hills that has no equivalent in Northern Mittoli cartographic convention. Outside travelers who have obtained descriptions of the hills from Thalmagar contacts report that the descriptions are entirely adequate for navigation and entirely impossible to transfer onto a map, because the Thalmagar system references relationships between features rather than absolute positions.

## The Thalmagar Seasonal Range

The Thalmagar occupy Cape Thalmagar as their primary territory but their range is not fixed to the cape. In the harshest seasons — the winter period when the cape's coastal exposure produces conditions that even the Thalmagar consider extreme — parts of the population move south and east into the Noth Hills, where the terrain provides wind shelter and the caves and overhangs of the hill outcrops offer a kind of protection unavailable on the exposed cape coast.

The Thalmagar's knowledge of the Noth Hills is the oldest such knowledge that exists for this territory in any form that has reached outside observers. It is not a seasonal familiarity but a generational inheritance: specific routes through the hills, specific shelter locations, specific water sources that do not freeze through even severe winters because of the upwelling geology beneath them, specific locations where the megafauna concentrate in particular seasons and that must be approached with the same careful protocol that governs all Thalmagar-megafauna interactions. This knowledge is not written. It is held in a form of oral-spatial memory that the Thalmagar transmit to their children as children learn the cape itself — by moving through the landscape in the company of people who know it.

What outside observers who have encountered Thalmagar groups in the Noth Hills note consistently is that the movement appears purposeful in a way that does not resemble searching. The people in the hills know where they are going. They are not discovering the route; they are following one. The hills to the Thalmagar are not wilderness territory in the sense that the outside world uses the term — unknown, unmapped, requiring exploratory orientation. They are familiar territory that happens to lack permanent settlement. The difference between these two things is not legible from outside without extended observation, and extended observation of Thalmagar groups in the hills is not a condition the Thalmagar have historically invited.

In the warmest months, the cape is the better territory: fishing, the Shore Elder beaches, the Rack-Wing cliffs, the Palmant and Hul whose seasonal ranges return the people and the large animals to shared ground. The Noth Hills in summer are used differently — for specific resources the cape does not provide, for transit, for the kind of movement that the open country above the treeline allows in a way the forest below does not. The hills are not the primary range; they are the secondary range, which means the Thalmagar know them less continuously than the cape but know them in the way that knowledge deepens when it is regularly tested against conditions that require accuracy.

## Megafauna Presence

The megafauna of the Thalmagar cape range is not confined to the cape. The **Long-Back**, specifically, uses the Noth Hills as part of a seasonal route that the Thalmagar track with the attention appropriate to an animal whose location is always relevant to the location of the people. The Long-Back's browsing range follows the distribution of specific upper-canopy tree species; in the Noth Hills this means the animal is most present where the landscape transitions toward the tundra edge, where the last of the forest trees grow thinly and the Long-Back can reach growth that is unavailable in the closed canopy below.

The Long-Back's passage through the hills leaves evidence that the Thalmagar read as a matter of practical attention: the specific trees that have been browsed to the limit of the Long-Back's reach, the trails pressed into the bog surface that the animal's weight creates and that last until freeze-up, the **displaced erratics** — smaller boulders moved from their settled positions by the animal's movement through confined outcrops passages, distinguishable from water-moved or ice-moved displacement by the direction and context.

The **Cape Palmant** does not regularly range into the Noth Hills in the same pattern as the Long-Back, but the tundra-edge of the hills sees Palmant movement in the transitional seasons when the herds shift between the cape's interior and the more open tundra. A large Palmant herd crossing the tundra-edge of the hills is visible, audible, and leaves evidence of passage in the flattened ground and the specific pattern of trampled vegetation that distinguishes a herd crossing from everything else that moves through the same ground.

The **Standing Hul** does not commonly range from the cape into the hills, but it is not absent. Individual animals — males establishing or testing range — have been recorded in the Noth Hills by Thalmagar accounts transmitted to outside traders. The probability of encounter is low enough not to constitute a regular planning consideration for the Thalmagar in the hills, but "low" is not "zero," and the Thalmagar do not make the mistake of treating low probability as impossible. The protocols for Hul encounter in the hills are the same as the protocols for Hul encounter anywhere: steady, calm, do not crowd the animal's options, give it a direction.

## The Cave Systems

The Noth Hills' outcrop geology produces cave systems as a consequence of the same processes that produced everything else in the landscape. Vertical jointing in the granites and metamorphics creates fissures; the fissures become passages where the rock has fractured at favorable angles; the passages become chambers where blocks have fallen and created spaces. These are not solution caves in the limestone sense — the Noth Hills do not have limestone — but they are regular enough and varied enough in size and character to constitute a distinct feature of the hill system.

The Thalmagar identify the cave systems by their own reference convention — not numbered or formally named, but placed in a spatial vocabulary of the hills that uses the outcrop landmarks and the drainage patterns as reference. The caves are known individually; which ones are useful for shelter, which ones have the specific internal flow of cold air that indicates connection to a deeper system, which ones show evidence of use by animals that cannot be assumed to be absent. The last is not a theoretical concern. The caves that are useful for shelter in the hills are the same caves that provide shelter to other things in the hills, and the caves are not always unoccupied when the Thalmagar arrive.

What the cave systems contain in the way of archaeological record — deposits from very long habitation, from Thalmagar seasonal use over many generations, from whatever was in the Noth Hills before the Thalmagar if anything was — is not established. The Caeras Valley Academy has proposed that the Noth Hills caves would be of extraordinary interest to deep-time investigators. The proposal has not produced access; the Thalmagar control the territory, and the Academy's interest in the caves has not met a corresponding Thalmagar interest in having the Academy in the hills.

The oldest cave deposits that the Thalmagar have described in accounts that have reached outside observers include material consistent with very long human presence — worked bone, charcoal layers, specific placements of erratic stones in patterns that suggest deliberate arrangement. Whether these are Thalmagar-origin materials from centuries of seasonal occupation, or materials from an earlier presence that the Thalmagar succeeded or absorbed or are independent of, is not established. The Thalmagar, asked about this, do not find the distinction as interesting as outside scholars do. The hills have been known as long as there has been anyone to know them. That is the answer they offer, and they appear to consider it complete.

## Access and the Absence of Roads

There is no road to the Noth Hills from the south. There is no road from the east or the west. The Thalmagar approach from the cape does not require roads because the people who use it do not organize their movement around road infrastructure; they organize it around terrain knowledge, which is not the same thing and does not produce the same residue in the landscape.

What this means in practice: the Noth Hills are inaccessible to anyone who relies on established routes and does not have either local guidance or the specific accumulated knowledge of the landscape that the Thalmagar hold. A northern Azhoran traveler heading for the Noth Hills from Henborth or the Lond plateau approaches them from the south through the Nothwood, which has its own orientation difficulties, and emerges into hill country that offers no marked entry points, no paths that identify themselves as paths, and no system of landmarks that maps onto the Northern Mittoli spatial vocabulary with which most such travelers would approach the problem.

The hill crests offer visibility, but visibility in the Noth Hills shows more hills of similar profile and similar height, and the outcrops that the Thalmagar use as reference points are not identifiable to an outsider as reference points specifically rather than simply as rocks. The bogs are not marked. The cave systems are not marked. The Long-Back's trails are identifiable to someone who knows what they are looking at and ambiguous to someone who does not.

The Noth Hills are not dangerous to a prepared traveler with appropriate cold-weather knowledge and the ability to navigate without external markers. They are dangerous to an unprepared traveler in the way that any northern upland is dangerous: cold, wet, wind-exposed at the crests, confusingly uniform in appearance, and capable of detaining a person who cannot find their way out longer than the person's supplies will support. The accounts of travelers who have entered the hills without Thalmagar guidance and returned without incident tend to report that they made their way through by luck and persistence, and the accounts of travelers who did not return are, necessarily, not available.

The absence of a road is not an oversight. It is the condition of the territory.
