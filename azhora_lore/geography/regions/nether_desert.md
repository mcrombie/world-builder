# Nether Desert

The Nether Desert is the dry plateau country of the upper Neth drainage — the high interior ground from which the Neth descends before it finds the basin that becomes the Nethermere. Where the lower Neth is defined by abundance, by the water that spreads and stays and supports the complicated life of the Nethrani and their seasonal management of the flood, the upper Neth country is defined by the opposite: thin soil, interrupted drainage, and a rainfall pattern that fails with enough regularity that nothing permanent has been built here in any generation that has left a record. The Nether Desert is what the Neth leaves behind when it goes downstream.

The name follows the Neth system rather than a separate topographical tradition. The inner-branch country names its marginal deserts after the rivers they border — the Oves Desert sits against the Oveth basin, the Nether Desert against the Neth — and the pattern holds even when, as here, the river's character lower down seems to argue against the association. That a river flowing from a desert should produce a marshland below is not a contradiction the Nethrani find unusual. The upland gives up the water; the lowland receives it. The desert makes the Nethermere possible, at one remove.

## Geography

The Nether Desert occupies the elevated interior plateau west and south of the Nethermere watershed's drainage divide. Its surface is a mix of exposed rock and thin, stony soil — the kind of terrain that accumulates water during heavy rainfall events and loses it almost immediately to evaporation and drainage into the fractured rock below. The vegetation cover in ordinary years is sparse: drought-adapted scrub in the drainage channels, bare rock and gravel on the higher exposures, a thin grass that appears after rain and disappears within a week of it.

The elevation is moderately high — higher than the Nethermere basin to its north, and higher than most of the inner-branch country. This elevation is the primary cause of the aridity: the moisture-bearing weather systems that cross the inner plateau lose most of their precipitation to the ridge country before reaching the Nether Desert's southern face, and the prevailing winds from the south have already crossed substantial dry ground before they arrive. The desert is not dramatic in the Moroshé sense — there are no canyon systems, no dune fields, no formations whose scale announces themselves. It is a quiet aridity: the kind of place that travelers pass through quickly and describe, afterward, as featureless.

The boundary with Nethereum is not a sharp line. Moving north and east from the desert into the basin country, the terrain gradually softens: the rock gives way to soil, the channels become more consistent, the vegetation shifts from the scrub of the high plateau to the grassland and marsh-edge of the Neth's middle course. The transition takes twenty miles in the dry season and looks different in a wet year, when the Nethermere's expansion pushes the productive zone further up the drainage and the desert's southern margin briefly supports a vegetation cover it does not otherwise show.

## Human Use

The Nether Desert does not support settled population. What moves through it does so in transit, and the communities that use it do so seasonally or opportunistically rather than as a permanent commitment to the terrain.

The pastoral communities of the inner plateau margins — primarily small groups with clan affiliations to the Nethrani and to the Mithala-adjacent peoples — move animals into the Nether Desert after the rains, when the brief grass is available and the water holes that form in the rock depressions have not yet dried. The timing of this movement is managed carefully and has been for as long as anyone has records of it; the good years are known, the bad years are known, and the knowledge of which is which is held by the families who have always made the crossing. The animals that graze the Nether Desert after rain are moved back down into the productive country before the water fails, and the timing of the return is as closely managed as the timing of the departure.

Caravan routes cross the Nether Desert rather than following its margins, because the direct line through the high plateau is shorter than the longer routes around it. The crossing is not severe by the standards of the Moroshé or even the Ganesh — a capable caravan party with water knows what it is doing and moves through in the time required — but the absence of reliable water and the exposure to the wind make it a crossing that is planned for rather than taken casually. The waterholes that form in the rock basins after rain are mapped, and their reliability in different weather years is part of the practical knowledge that guides cross-plateau movement.

The Nethrani have no particular attachment to the Nether Desert. It is the country above them. It sends them water and takes nothing back. They name the weather that arrives from the south — the dry wind that drops off the plateau edge and crosses the Nethermere in late summer — the **nethwind**, and they regard it as the desert's exhale: the thing the upland country gives back after the water has passed through it. This is not mystical. It is descriptive.

## Relationship to Nethereum

The Nether Desert and the Nethermere are the two ends of the same system, and the Nethrani understand them as such. The high plateau holds no water permanently; it sheds what it receives into the channels that become the Neth, and the Neth delivers it to the basin below. In a year when the upland has received significant rain, the Nethermere floods high. In a drought year in the Nether Desert, the Nethermere is a smaller, quieter lake, and the Nethrani pull in their margin farming and wait.

This dependence is not symmetrical. The Nether Desert does not depend on the Nethermere. The Nethrani, however, are aware that the country above them matters to the country they live in, and the herding families who move between the two zones carry information about upland conditions that reaches the Nethrani before the water itself does. The reports of the herders — how much rain fell in the high country, how quickly the water holes refilled, how the seasonal grass timed against previous years — are taken seriously in the Nethrani flood-preparation tradition, because they are the earliest available evidence of what the coming flood season will be.
