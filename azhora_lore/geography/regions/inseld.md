# Inseld

The **Inseld** islands lie off the northern Azhoran coast in cold water — north of the main landmass and exposed to the open ocean that connects, in the Azhoran geographical imagination, to the far northern passage where the Ice Spear becomes visible on clear days. **East Inseld** and **West Inseld** are the two larger islands of a broken chain that extends northwest from the continental shelf's northern edge; between and around them, the skerries and submerged rocks that extend the navigational hazard significantly beyond the named islands make any approach to the chain an exercise in current knowledge and sea-reading. The Inseld communities have been here long enough that the cold is not a description of conditions but simply a description of the place — the same way that a valley is a valley. Their fishing is excellent for the specific species that cold deep water produces, their communities are small and self-sufficient, and they are administered by no mainland power in any form that either side would describe as functional administration.

---

## Geography and the Northern Waters

The two named islands sit on a shelf that drops abruptly to deep water on their northern and western sides. This shelf edge is where the cold-water upwelling occurs that makes the surrounding fishery productive: nutrient-rich deep water brought up by the mixing at the shelf break sustains the food chain that produces the specific fish species — **cold-shelf cod**, several species of large flatfish, and the deepwater species that go by local names with no Mittoli equivalents — that the Inseld communities harvest. The upwelling is consistent across seasons in a way that the Drent coast fishery is not; the deep cold does not warm in summer or thin in winter in the dramatic way of shallower coastal waters. The Inseld fishing is stable.

The approach from the Azhoran coast requires crossing the open northern shelf water — a passage of sufficient exposure that the weather conditions between the mainland and the islands matter as much as the destination conditions. In good weather the crossing is straightforward; in bad weather it is the kind of crossing that a competent northern-sea boat handles and that visitors from temperate latitudes find educational. The Inseld communities have boats built for the specific sea conditions of their environment: broad-beamed, high-sided, designed for the short steep swell that the northern shelf produces and for the cold that makes prolonged exposure during gear-setting and -hauling into a material consideration.

East and West Inseld are separated by a strait whose width — perhaps eight miles at its narrowest — is navigable in summer conditions and complicated in winter by ice drift from the northern ocean. In the deep winter months, pack ice occasionally reaches as far south as the Inseld strait, not consolidating but moving through, making the passage between the islands unreliable for weeks at a time. The communities on each island are sufficiently self-contained that this winter isolation is a managed condition rather than an emergency; they stock through summer and autumn specifically against the periods when inter-island contact becomes difficult or impossible.

The **Outer Skerries** — the collective term the Riesov traders use for the exposed rocks east of East Inseld and north of West Inseld — are the navigational hazard that gives the archipelago its worst reputation in Azhoran coastal records. The skerries do not appear on most Azhoran charts in reliable form, partly because they shift slightly in their above-water extent with the seasons and partly because the records are compiled from coastal pilots who have not spent enough time at Inseld to give useful detail. The Inseld communities know the skerries individually and navigate among them without difficulty. They do not publish the knowledge.

## The Cold-Water Fishery

The Inseld fishery is the reason for the islands' permanent habitation and, in practical terms, the reason for most of the contact between the islands and the mainland. The cold-shelf species that Inseld produces are not found in the same quality or quantity on the Drent coast or the Riesov coast to the south and east; the specific conditions of the shelf-edge upwelling, the depth of the cold water, and the food chain it sustains are particular to the Inseld zone and its nearest northern equivalents.

The **cold-shelf cod** of Inseld — salt-dried and exported south in quantity — has a commercial reputation in the Iberos coast markets that exceeds the product's volume in the trade. The specific density of the flesh, produced by cold water that requires the species to maintain more fat against temperature, is distinct from the temperate-water equivalents, and buyers who have handled both can distinguish them. The Inseld communities price their cod accordingly. They do not bargain much, because they have an accurate sense of what their product is worth and they have been selling to the Riesov traders long enough to know what the market will bear.

The deepwater flatfish — which the communities call **vethrek** in their local speech, a term that has no confident Mittoli equivalent in common use — is a seasonal species taken in quantity during the late summer runs when it rises toward the shelf edge. The vethrek is eaten fresh on the islands in ways that the dried-export version cannot represent and is exported salted in forms that arrive on the mainland in recognizable condition if the journey is short enough. The Riesov traders handle the timing of vethrek consignments with more attention than they give to most of their other goods, because the quality window for getting it to market in useful condition is narrow.

The Inseld communities do not fish to commercial scale alone. The fishery is also their food supply, and the management of which grounds are fished, at what intensity, and in what season is a community matter that the Pilot and Fisher Councils handle with the seriousness of people for whom a depleted ground is not a commercial problem but an existential one. The fishing effort on the outer grounds — the high-yield shelf-edge locations — is regulated by community agreement in ways that outside observers have occasionally noticed and reported without fully understanding. The community does not explain the regulation because explaining it to people who will not honor it produces no benefit.

## The Two Islands: East and West

East Inseld is the more commercially engaged of the two. It sits closer to the main crossing route from the Riesov coast and has the larger and better-sheltered harbor — a natural bay on its southeastern face that the community has improved with a stone breakwater maintained over generations. The settlement on East Inseld is large enough to maintain a permanent smithing operation, a boat-building yard, and the storage facilities necessary for the volume of dried fish that moves through in the export seasons. The population is perhaps six hundred across the island, with the majority in the main settlement.

**West Inseld** is smaller, colder, and less visited by outside traders. It sits further from the mainland crossing, its harbor is less sheltered, and its winter exposure to the open northern ocean is more severe than East Inseld's. The community on West Inseld is correspondingly more self-sufficient and less commercially integrated: it fishes, it maintains itself, and it sends fish south with the East Inseld boats when the inter-island contact allows. The West Inseld people are regarded by the East Inseld community with the specific warmth that neighboring communities with different characters but shared conditions tend to develop — not identical, but comprehending.

The two communities share some family connections from historical intermarriage, share the management of the strait fishery through an agreement maintained by custom rather than any formal document, and share the **Inseld Council** — a joint body that meets twice yearly when inter-island conditions permit, to handle matters that affect both islands. The Council does not administer daily life on either island; it handles disputes between the two communities and coordinates on external matters, particularly the terms set for outside traders. This is the minimum necessary structure for two small communities in shared circumstances, and it is exactly the structure that exists.

## Community and Governance

The Inseld communities govern themselves through a council of household heads that meets to handle disputes, make community decisions about fishing grounds and trade terms, and manage the relationship with outside parties. The council is not formally defined in any written document — the communities do not maintain written records in any substantial form, relying on a strong oral record-keeping tradition — and its authority derives from the community's recognition of it rather than from any external legitimation.

The most respected figures in the Inseld communities are not the council chairs or any equivalent of nobility but the **master fishers** — the older experienced operators who know the outer grounds best and whose judgment on fishing conditions, weather reading, and ground management is treated as authoritative. Their standing in community deliberations on fishing-related matters is more than advisory: a master fisher's assessment of a proposed change to ground management is not technically binding but is, in practice, followed.

The communities' approach to outside authority — to the Riesov traders who arrive with commercial interest, to the occasional Feradom vessel that comes north, to the administrative attention that mainland powers have intermittently paid — is one of polite unavailability for the purposes others have in mind. Not hostile. Not evasive in an elaborate sense. Simply arranged in a way that makes most outside administrative purposes difficult to pursue: no clear single authority to negotiate with, no written records to audit, no formal treaty structure to invoke. When the Riesov traders ask whether the Inseld communities are willing to pay any form of tribute to the Riesov lords, the answer is a variant of "we pay in cod." This is a joke. It is also an accurate statement of the actual arrangement.

## The Riesov and Witherst Connections

The **Riesov** are the Inseld communities' primary mainland commercial connection. Riesov traders cross to East Inseld on the summer and autumn schedule — when the crossing is manageable and the dried-fish stockpile is ready to move — and the relationship between the two communities has accumulated over enough generations to have a character of its own. The Riesov know the Inseld communities well enough not to try to administer them and the Inseld communities find the Riesov a reliable buyer. The terms of trade are known and approximately fair; neither party attempts to renegotiate dramatically from year to year.

The Riesov maintain a seasonal presence at East Inseld — a warehouse and trading-house that Riesov traders occupy in the commercial seasons and leave empty in winter — rather than a permanent presence. This is the Riesov's practical preference as well as the Inseld community's: a permanent outside presence creates complications that a seasonal one avoids.

The **Witherst** connection is different in character. The Witherst — the people of the White Hills coast to the west — have an eight-week window in the Gorgi Mountains that determines their annual contact with the wider Azhoran continent, and the Inseld trade is one of the routes they have developed for that window. Witherst vessels reach East Inseld during the commercial season, and the relationship is less regularized than the Riesov trade — the Witherst bring goods from their western coast that are not otherwise available in the Inseld supply chain: specific amber varieties, sea ivory, the salt-preserved fish of a different fishery. The Inseld communities trade cold-shelf cod and dried flatfish for these. The Witherst find the Inseld cold-water products useful for their own coastal trade. Both communities find the exchange valuable enough to maintain.

The Witherst's relationship to the Inseld is not mediated through the Riesov, which is a deliberate preference of both the Witherst and the Inseld communities. The Riesov would not mind being the intermediary. The other parties have found that direct contact works better for them.

## Sea Routes

The Inseld islands sit at the northern edge of the Azhoran maritime world in a geographic position that makes them a logical waypoint for any vessel attempting the northern passage — the route that passes along the Azhoran northern coast and continues west into the open ocean beyond the continent's end. Whether such routes are regularly used is not clearly documented in the Azhoran record; the northern passage remains more navigational tradition than commercial reality. But the Inseld community's awareness of northern conditions — the ice movement, the weather patterns from the north, the behavior of the specific currents that reach the islands from the open ocean — exceeds what any other Azhoran community possesses, and the value of this awareness to anyone seriously contemplating the northern passage is obvious.

What the Inseld communities know about conditions further north, in the direction of the Ice Spear, has not been formally recorded in any Academy document. They have not been asked in ways they found worth answering, or they have been asked and have answered in ways that did not transfer well into the question's framing. The Academy's northern geographic records note the Inseld communities as sources of northern-condition information and note the difficulty of translating that information into chart-useful form. The communities' navigational knowledge is embedded in their experience of being here, not abstracted into transferable data. This is a distinction that matters for what kind of knowledge can leave the islands and what kind cannot.
