# Aurumlis Archipelago

The **Aurumlis Archipelago** lies in the southern ocean off Azhora's southwestern coast — warmer water than the Bouén peninsula to the northeast, with a southern exposure that gives the islands a climate closer to the transitional character of Trogo than to the cold current that defines Bouén proper. The name contains the Boueni root *aur-* — sea-light, or gold-light, or southern shimmer, depending on which Boueni speaker is translating and in what context — and this linguistic fact places these islands within the Boueni navigational tradition even though the Boueni homeland is far to the northeast. The implication — that the Aurumlis were reached during Boueni crossing attempts at some period prior to the current documentation — is neither confirmed nor denied by Boueni navigators when asked directly. The standard response is a specific silence: not hostile, not evasive, but the silence of people for whom the question has a known answer they have not decided to share. The islands are used. They have been used. By whom, from where, in what period, and through what routes are questions that administrative records of adjacent mainland territories cannot answer because the Aurumlis do not fall within any adjacent mainland territory's administration.

## Geography

The archipelago is scattered rather than chained — not a linear barrier-island formation like the Peblos or a loose but directional grouping like the Azhor Stones, but a broader dispersal of islands and outcrops across perhaps one hundred and fifty miles of open water, with no clear organizing axis that a chart-maker would find satisfying. The larger islands cluster loosely in the archipelago's northern half; the southern half grades into scattered rocks and exposed reefs that are navigational hazards in unfamiliar water and, for navigators who know the system, a set of reference points reliable enough to work from.

The geology is older than volcanic. The main islands are formed from a combination of grey schist and folded sedimentary rock, the latter containing fossil sequences that Academy natural historians who have visited the northern edges of the archipelago describe as significant for the reconstruction of the southern ocean's geological history. The rock does not yield good agricultural soil — the thin soils on the island interiors support low scrub, some grassland on the better-drained slopes, and almost nothing that a cultivating farmer from the Mittoli plains would recognize as productive land. What the islands have is position and, on the larger examples, fresh water.

The largest island — referred to in Azhoran records by the Mittolified approximation **Aurem** — is about twelve miles across at its widest, with a central ridgeline high enough to generate its own rainfall. The ridgeline is covered in dense, low-growing vegetation that Academy botanists have noted contains several species found nowhere else in their documented range — isolated populations, they suggest, of plants that were once distributed more widely across the southern island systems before the sea levels that created the current archipelago configuration cut them off. The Boueni, when shown Academy botanical records of Aurem species, recognize some of them by name. They do not always specify where they know those names from.

The smaller islands are more various. Some are elevated enough to hold seasonal water. Some are flat enough that storm surge overtops them entirely. Two of the southern outcrops are sea-stack formations — vertical-sided columns of rock too narrow to land on and too dramatic to ignore — that function as navigation fixes for passages through the archipelago's more complex southern section. These stacks have names in Boueni that do not translate literally but whose functional meaning is something like "the one that tells you where you are."

## The Boueni Connection

The presence of the Boueni root *aur-* in the archipelago's name is the most direct evidence of Boueni prior knowledge of these islands, and it is the kind of evidence that requires interpretation rather than simply recording. The Boueni navigational tradition is oral and geographically encoded — names carry information about position, conditions, and navigational significance in ways that outside scholars have found partially recoverable but not fully transparent. A root meaning "sea-light" or "southern shimmer" applied to an island chain in the southern ocean is consistent with Boueni naming practice for landmasses reached during extended open-ocean crossings, where the visual phenomena associated with island presence — the lightening of the sky over land, the specific refraction of sunlight from shallow water — serve as navigation signals.

The Boueni do not claim administrative sovereignty over the Aurumlis. They do not claim anything formally. What they have is relationship: a navigational relationship, an ecological knowledge relationship, and what appears to be a longstanding commercial relationship of a kind that predates any Azhoran documentation. Boueni vessels transit the Aurumlis regularly, in both directions — south from the Bouén coast toward the archipelago and beyond, and north from the islands back toward the Azhoran mainland coast. They do not advertise the routes they take or the purposes of these transits, and the communities of the Aurumlis — whoever they are, and the question is not as simple as it looks — do not advertise the Boueni presence to outside inquirers.

The Azhoran documentation of the Aurumlis begins with what appear to be secondhand accounts of Boueni reports, filtered through the commercial networks of the southwestern Azhoran coast cities. These accounts are consistent in noting the islands' existence and position and inconsistent in almost everything else — the number of islands, the character of the inhabitants, whether there is a permanent population or merely seasonal use. The inconsistency is exactly what would result from accounts that were never meant to be complete. The Boueni who provided the information provided enough for the accounts to exist and not more.

## Ecology

What is documented about the Aurumlis ecology comes from the few Azhoran visits to the northern edge of the archipelago and from the Boueni reports — incomplete, selectively shared — that have entered the Azhoran record. The larger islands support substantial seabird populations, and the coastal waters are rich in the pelagic species that open southern ocean supports — larger, in some cases, than the equivalent northern species, in the way that southern sea creatures frequently are. The reef formations in the shallower water between the northern islands follow patterns that an observer familiar with the Azhor Stones would recognize: coral-built structures in the warmer water, with the specific community of species that reef environments sustain in the southern ocean zone.

Several of the plant species found in the Aurumlis interior have what appear to be domesticated relatives in the southern archipelago peoples' cultivated areas — a pattern that suggests either common origin or long-term exchange. The direction of exchange, if it happened, is not established from the botanical evidence alone. Some plants move northward in trade. Some cultivated forms are selections from wild material that was never found anywhere near Azhora. The Aurumlis, in this reading, might be a secondary distribution point for a southern botanical tradition that reached the islands through the same people who brought the *aur-* root to their name.

The sea turtle populations of the Aurumlis are large and use the beaches of several of the larger islands for nesting in quantities that Azhoran visitors have described as striking — more concentrated than the Azhor Stones populations, more undisturbed. Whether the low disturbance reflects human restraint or simply low human presence is a question the documentation does not resolve.

## The Navigation Routes

The Aurumlis sits at a position that makes it relevant to several different maritime route systems simultaneously — and the ambiguity of which route system claims it is, in practice, the source of its navigational significance. A vessel leaving the southwestern Azhoran coast heading south passes the Aurumlis or near it; a vessel continuing further south will find the archipelago useful as a last provisioning point before open ocean. A vessel crossing from the west — from whatever lies in that direction — finds the Aurumlis at the end of a crossing of significant distance. A vessel moving northeast from the southern archipelago finds the Aurumlis as a waypoint on the approach toward Azhoran waters.

All of these routes are plausible. Boueni navigational practice accounts for all of them. The routes that are documented in Azhoran records are only the ones that Azhoran vessels have used, which is to say: the southward coastal route from the Bouén coast, continuing to the Aurumlis and occasionally beyond. The other routes exist — the movement patterns of Boueni vessels that do not begin from the Bouén coast and do not end at Azhoran ports demonstrate this — but they are not in the Azhoran record because no Azhoran record-keeper was there.

The **Western Crossing**, as Boueni navigators refer to it in contexts where they refer to it at all, is the route that connects the Aurumlis to islands further west across an open stretch of ocean. The Academy has no charts of what lies west of the Aurumlis. The Boueni have not provided charts. The fact that Boueni vessels transit the Aurumlis from and toward the west — observed by the Azhoran commercial agents in the southwestern coast cities who note vessel arrivals and departures — is in the record. The destination is not.

## Adjacent Sea-Peoples

The question of who uses the Aurumlis, and from where, involves at least three different communities whose relationship to the islands cannot be reduced to any single administrative description. The Boueni presence has been discussed. The southern archipelago peoples — who appear in the Aurumlis context as occasional transitors, apparently moving on their own north-south routes that graze the archipelago's southern edge — have a presence the Azhoran record documents thinly and without much detail. And there is the possibility, raised by the cistern structures and middens on the larger islands, that there is or was a community more permanently associated with the islands themselves.

The southern archipelago peoples who appear at the Aurumlis are recognizably related to the peoples documented at the Azhor Stones, but the specific relationship — same people, connected network, or distinct populations with a shared southern navigational tradition — is not established. They use the reef waters. They trade with whoever is on the islands when they arrive. They do not, as far as any Azhoran observer has documented, treat the archipelago as anyone else's territory or as their own in any exclusive sense.

The Boueni relationship to all of this is, characteristically, more presence than statement. Boueni vessels at the Aurumlis interact with southern peoples at the Aurumlis in ways that suggest established protocol — forms of exchange and acknowledgment that are not improvised on arrival. How old this protocol is, and who established it first, is not something the Boueni have entered into the record.

## What Is Known and What Is Not

The Aurumlis Archipelago is one of the regions where the limits of Azhoran geographical knowledge become structurally visible. Azhoran knowledge of the archipelago is bounded at every edge by the limits of what interested parties have been willing to share — the Boueni share enough for the islands' existence and general position to be recorded, and not more; the southern peoples' route information has not been shared with Azhoran record-keepers; the permanent residents, if there are permanent residents in any sustained sense, have not invited outside documentation.

What the Azhoran record contains: the islands' existence, their approximate position southwest of the Bouén coast, some botanical and ecological data from northern-edge visits, the Boueni root in the name, the presence of cistern structures suggesting prior organized habitation, and the consistent transit of Boueni vessels whose routes extend beyond the Azhoran record's horizon.

What the Azhoran record does not contain: reliable population data, the character of current habitation if any, the western routes that Boueni vessels take to and from the archipelago, the nature of the arrangement between Boueni and southern peoples at the islands, and whatever relationship the Aurumlis has to the communities and routes that exist south and west of Azhoran documented space.

The Academy's Geographical Survey maintains an entry for the Aurumlis as a "partially documented island group in the southwestern ocean, position and extent approximate." This is accurate. It is also a description that could remain accurate indefinitely, because the parties who hold the fuller information have not found Azhoran documentation of it useful enough to provide it, and the Azhoran exploratory capacity to reach the archipelago independently and work through it systematically has not been mounted in any sustained form. The Aurumlis will continue to be used by the people who use it. The Azhoran record will continue to know roughly where it is and very little else. Both of these things appear to suit the people who use the archipelago adequately.
